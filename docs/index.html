Private Markets GP Stakes Fundraising Fee Structures

GP stakes just beat PE at its own game. The fee stream is the new alpha.

  • Arctos — the GP-stakes and sports-team investor being acquired by KKR — closed a $6.2 billion fund, the largest ever raised solely to buy minority stakes in private equity and alternative asset managers themselves
  • The median primary buyout fund now takes 22 months to close, while first-time fund counts are down sharply — yet this meta-layer fund closed at record size
  • Institutional investors are effectively voting that the management fee and carried interest of top firms are a safer bet than the underlying buyout returns those firms generate

The take

Arctos just closed a $6.2 billion fund — the largest ever raised — dedicated to buying minority stakes in private equity and alternative managers themselves. Arctos, for the uninitiated, is the GP-stakes and sports-team specialist that KKR agreed to acquire earlier this cycle. So the story here isn't "another big fund closed." It's that in a market where LPs are choking off primary buyout fundraising and the median fund takes 22 months to close, the fastest capital formation is happening one layer up the stack — financing the managers, not the deals.

Here's the tell everyone is skipping past: this is a fund that buys the general partner, not the portfolio. Think about what that means. LPs have decided that the surest return in private equity is no longer the underlying companies — it's the fee stream and carried interest of the firm collecting the money. When the smartest capital in the room stops betting on the asset class and starts betting on the toll booth, that's a verdict on where the alpha actually sits. The asset is the management fee. The 2-and-20 is the moat.

The consensus reads GP stakes as a liquidity solution — a way for aging founders to monetize and fund succession. That's the polite version, and it's half true. The honest version is that GP-stakes buyers are underwriting permanence. They are betting that the incumbent brand-name managers — Apollo, KKR, the same names raising $25-30B flagships — have become so entrenched in institutional allocations that their fee streams are effectively annuities regardless of what the underlying vintages actually return. You are buying the distribution monopoly, not the investing skill. Those are very different things, and the price implies they're the same.

Second-order effect nobody is pricing: this creates a structural conflict that will surface in the next down cycle. When a GP-stakes fund owns a piece of your management company, the manager's incentive to raise the next fund — to keep the fee engine turning — hardens into an obligation to its own equity holders. Fund size stops being a function of opportunity and becomes a function of the balance sheet's need to service the stakes buyer. That's how you get the next $20B fund that shouldn't exist. AUM growth becomes the product, and returns become the marketing.

For a Singapore allocator, the read-across is simple. If Arctos can raise $6.2B to own the managers while first-time funds are down and closings are stretching to two years, the market is telling you the fee is safer than the return. Fine — then price your primary commitments accordingly, and stop paying founder-economics premiums to firms that just sold their own upside to somebody smarter than you.

The other side

GP-stakes funds are not immune to the cycle they think they've escaped. If LP appetite for private equity cools structurally — not just the current squeeze but a multi-year rotation — AUM growth at the underlying managers slows, fee revenue compresses, and the 'annuity' thesis gets stress-tested in a hurry. Arctos and peers like Blue Owl's GP stakes platform (Blue Owl is a large alternative asset manager that runs one of the biggest competing stakes businesses) are pricing these acquisitions at multiples that assume perpetual fundraising success. A prolonged distribution drought, where buyout funds can't return capital and LPs stop re-upping, would impair those fee streams faster than the current purchase multiples reflect.

The mechanics of why this is happening are worth slowing down on. A GP-stakes fund acquires a minority ownership interest in the management company of an alternative asset manager — the entity that collects the annual management fee, typically 1.5 to 2 percent of committed capital, regardless of how the underlying investments perform. That fee is not contingent on exits, public markets, or even whether the firm is generating returns. It runs as long as the fund is within its investment period and follow-on management window, which for a large buyout fund can be a decade or more. Stack three or four successive funds on top of each other, each paying its own management fee, and you have a cash flow profile that looks less like private equity and more like a toll road.

Arctos's $6.2 billion close is the market putting a very large number on how durable those toll roads look right now. For context, the prior record for a GP-stakes fund was set by Blue Owl's GP Stakes platform at around $12 billion across its combined vehicles — but the single-fund record now belongs to Arctos, and it closed in an environment where most managers raising primary capital are struggling. That divergence is the signal. Capital is not leaving private markets broadly; it is migrating to the part of private markets that doesn't require you to underwrite a specific portfolio company, a specific vintage year, or a specific exit environment.

The historical parallel worth reaching for here is the wave of financial exchange demutualization in the late 1990s and early 2000s. When the NYSE and Nasdaq converted from member-owned cooperatives to shareholder-owned public companies, sophisticated investors quickly figured out that owning the exchange was a better business than trading on it. Volume risk was real but manageable; the structural position of the exchange — every trade had to clear through it — meant the fee income was close to inescapable. GP stakes is structurally analogous. The biggest alternative managers have embedded themselves so deeply in institutional portfolio construction that replacing them requires a governance process, an RFP, a board vote, and a reallocation cycle that takes years. That friction is the moat, and Arctos is buying it.

The second-order tension this creates is generational. Many of the GPs selling stakes to funds like Arctos are founder-led firms where the founding partner is approaching or past sixty. The stated rationale is succession planning and balance sheet liquidity — the founder monetizes some equity, the firm gets capital to seed new strategies, everyone wins. But the unstated dynamic is that Arctos now has an equity interest in the management company continuing to grow AUM. A founder who might otherwise wind down gracefully, return capital, and let a smaller successor fund reflect a smaller opportunity set now has an institutional equity holder with a financial interest in the opposite outcome. The incentive to raise the next fund, even when the vintage timing is poor, becomes structural rather than optional. That is not a hypothetical risk — it is the predictable output of the ownership structure, and it will show up in fund sizes during the next decade.

Private Markets GP-Led Secondaries LP Dynamics Liquidity

Continuation funds are tripling because the exit market is broken. Schroders is calling that progress.

  • Schroders Capital projects GP-led secondaries — fund structures where a PE sponsor sells an asset to a new pool it also controls — will grow from $109bn in 2025 to over $330bn by 2035
  • More than two-thirds of the deals Schroders evaluated between 2022 and 2024 involved companies valued below $750m, where price discovery is easier and outside buyers do exist
  • Schroders identifies the 'exit gap' — the mismatch between when sponsors need to return cash and when markets will pay full price — as one of five structural drivers, implicitly confirming the IPO and M&A routes aren't recovering

The take

Schroders Capital just published research forecasting that continuation vehicles — fund structures that let a private equity sponsor hold a winning asset past a fund's normal ten-year life by selling it to a new pool of investors — will more than triple from $109bn in 2025 to over $330bn by 2035. The framing is celebratory: continuation funds have "moved beyond being a temporary solution," in the words of CIO Nils Rode, and are now a "core feature." Read that sentence again, because it's an admission dressed as a milestone.

Here's what's actually going on. A continuation fund is a GP buying an asset from itself, setting the price on both sides of the trade, and charging fees to do it. When Schroders says this is now "structural, not cyclical," what they mean is that the exit market is permanently broken and the industry has institutionalized the workaround. The IPO window is shut, strategic buyers are disciplined, and sponsor-to-sponsor deals get valuation scrutiny — so the last liquidity option is to sell the company to a fund you also control. That's not value creation. That's a GP refusing to mark to a real bid and calling it conviction.

The tell is in the numbers Schroders is proud of. Two-thirds of the deals they like are sub-$750m enterprise value, in the lower mid-market. Translation: the assets clearing at fair prices are the small, boring, financeable ones. The $2bn+ trophies that need continuation vehicles are precisely the ones that can't find an outside buyer at the carrying mark, which is exactly why the sponsor is buying them from itself. The demand is structural because the alternative — a real exit at a real clearing price — would print DPI numbers that vaporize the 2021 vintage's paper marks.

For an allocator, the discipline is simple and unpopular. When a continuation vehicle crosses your desk, your first question is not "what's the upside," it's "who set this price and why couldn't they sell to anyone else." A genuine continuation deal has a fresh, independent, arm's-length lead investor writing a large check and negotiating hard. A laundering exercise has a GP rolling its own carry, a thin syndicate, and a price suspiciously close to the last mark. The spread between those two is the entire game, and the $330bn forecast guarantees far more of the second kind than the first.

The uncomfortable read-across: an industry that needs to triple the size of its self-dealing mechanism over a decade is telling you its traditional exit engine is not coming back. Schroders is selling you the shovel. Just be clear you're buying it because the mine caved in.

The other side

The strongest version of the bull case is that continuation vehicles are genuinely better alignment, not worse. If a GP has a compounding asset — a software business growing 20% annually, say — forcing a sale at year ten because the fund clock ran out destroys value for everyone. The original structure was arbitrary, not optimal. Continuation funds let the best operators keep running their best companies, and the data bears this out: top-quartile continuation deals have posted returns competitive with traditional buyouts, with lower dispersion. The problem isn't the structure — it's that a good structure will inevitably attract bad actors, and distinguishing between the two requires diligence most LPs aren't staffed to do.

Say more →

The $330bn forecast deserves to be held next to a number Schroders doesn't publish: the estimated $3 trillion in unrealized private equity NAV sitting on sponsor books globally, much of it marked at prices that have not been stress-tested by an arm's-length transaction. Against that backdrop, tripling the continuation market isn't a growth story — it's a pressure-relief valve being quietly widened before the boiler blows.

The mechanism matters here. A traditional private equity exit — IPO, strategic sale, sponsor-to-sponsor — involves a buyer who owes nothing to the seller and will negotiate hard on price. A continuation vehicle, in its worst form, involves the same GP sitting on both sides of the table, hiring a third-party valuation firm that it also pays, and presenting existing limited partners (the pension funds, endowments, and sovereign wealth funds that supplied the original capital) with a binary choice: roll your position into the new vehicle at this price, or take liquidity at this price. The price, in both cases, is the one the GP prefers. That's not a market. That's a menu with one item.

The secondary market intermediaries — placement agents and advisory firms like Lazard, Evercore, and a handful of specialists like Jefferies — will tell you that competitive continuation processes with genuine tension do exist and do produce fair outcomes. That's true. It's also true that as volume triples, the share of genuinely competitive processes will shrink relative to the total. There simply aren't enough sophisticated secondary buyers with the capital, the diligence capacity, and the willingness to walk away from a bad deal to absorb $330bn a year with discipline. When demand for the product exceeds the supply of rigorous buyers, pricing power shifts back to the seller — who, in this case, is also the buyer.

The lower mid-market focus Schroders champions is worth examining on its own terms. Companies below $750m in enterprise value are genuinely easier to finance and do attract a broader buyer set. But they're also the assets where a GP's informational advantage over a prospective outside buyer is largest — private financials, customer concentration data, and management dependencies that a sponsor has spent years learning are exactly the variables an incoming buyer can't verify quickly. That information asymmetry is a feature of the deal for the GP, not a coincidence.

Historically, the closest parallel is the collateralized debt obligation market of the mid-2000s, which also began as a genuinely useful financial innovation — a way to redistribute risk and provide liquidity — and became a systemic problem only when volume outran the diligence capacity of the buyers absorbing it. The CDO market didn't collapse because the structure was inherently fraudulent. It collapsed because at scale, the incentive to manufacture product overwhelmed the incentive to ensure quality. Continuation vehicles aren't CDOs, and private equity isn't leveraged mortgage lending. But the dynamic — a useful tool becoming a volume game where price discipline erodes as the market grows — is recognizable.

The allocator's practical problem is that saying no to continuation vehicles entirely means forgoing the genuine ones, and the genuine ones can be excellent risk-adjusted investments. The answer isn't abstention. It's building the internal capability to tell the difference, which means staffing secondary diligence teams, demanding co-investment rights as a condition of rolling, and requiring independent lead investors with real capital at risk before signing anything. Most LP offices aren't built for that. Schroders, which runs a secondaries program of its own, is betting they won't build it in time.

Revolut's US move is a payments infrastructure play. The challenger-bank framing is a decoy.

  • Revolut filed for a US national bank charter in March 2026, backed by a $500 million commitment and a new US CEO, Cetin Duransoy, hired specifically to run the American buildout.
  • Three European neobanks — Monzo, N26, and Bunq — all attempted US expansions and retreated; Revolut is pressing ahead at a $75 billion valuation.
  • The charter application is bundled with a stablecoin pitch timed to the GENIUS Act regulatory framework, positioning Revolut to issue dollar-denominated stablecoins under a federally supervised structure.

The take

Revolut filed for a US national bank charter in March 2026 and is backing the American push with $500 million and a new US CEO, Cetin Duransoy, pulled in to run the buildout. What makes this notable isn't the charter application — plenty of fintechs have chased one — it's the graveyard Revolut is walking into. Monzo, N26, and Bunq all planted flags in the US and pulled them out. Revolut is doing it anyway, at a $75B valuation, which means either they know something the last three didn't, or they're about to donate a very expensive lesson to the same headstones.

Here's the contrarian read: the charter isn't the story, and neither is the consumer app. Everyone's evaluating this as "can Revolut win US retail customers," which is the wrong frame — that's the fight Monzo and N26 lost, and there's no reason to think Revolut's neon card wins where JPMorgan, Chime, and Cash App already own the funnel. The tell is the stablecoin pitch bundled into the charter application. Revolut isn't trying to be an American bank. It's trying to become the settlement rail for cross-border and multi-currency flow, and the US charter is the regulatory key that unlocks dollar stablecoin issuance under GENIUS-era rules. That's a payments infrastructure play wearing a challenger-bank costume.

Why this matters for capital allocation: the multi-currency, remittance-heavy, internationally-minded customer is the one segment US incumbents genuinely underserve, because their cost structure was built for domestic checking accounts, not for someone moving money between six currencies a month. Revolut already dominates this behavior in Europe and has 50M+ users globally who cross borders. If they can attach dollar stablecoin settlement to that existing flow, they're not competing with Chime — they're competing with the correspondent banking system and the SWIFT toll booth. The margin there is structural, not promotional, which is exactly what the dead European challengers never had. They burned cash buying domestic customers who had no reason to switch.

The risk nobody's pricing is timing and regulatory whiplash. A national charter takes 18-24 months minimum, and the stablecoin regime that makes this thesis work is one administration away from being reworked. Revolut is committing $500M into a two-front build — charter approval plus stablecoin rails — where both fronts depend on Washington cooperating on a schedule Washington doesn't keep.

For allocators holding this through ICONIQ's Strategic Partners VII: the mark won't move on US retail account growth, and don't let the quarterly deck tell you it should. Watch stablecoin transaction volume and the charter's approval calendar. If the charter clears and the rail attaches, the international-flow franchise reprices upward. If it stalls, you're funding the fourth headstone — just a more expensive one.

The other side

The retail foothold still matters, and dismissing it may be too clever. Stablecoin rails need transaction volume to be worth anything, and transaction volume requires users — which means Revolut does have to win some share of the domestic customer funnel to seed the network, not just inherit its European base. The GENIUS Act framework for stablecoin issuance is also newer and less tested than it looks; a chartered stablecoin issuer will still face state money-transmitter requirements, Fed master account politics, and a regulatory examination apparatus that has historically moved at a pace that kills momentum-dependent fintechs. The graveyard argument cuts both ways: those prior failures weren't just strategic errors, they were also evidence that US regulators and US consumers are structurally resistant to foreign-headquartered challengers in ways that $500 million doesn't automatically solve.

Say more →

Start with the structural problem the dead challengers never solved. Monzo, N26, and Bunq each entered the US running the same playbook: a slick mobile interface, no-fee foreign transactions, and the implicit pitch that American banking is broken and Europeans fixed it. The playbook failed not because the product was bad but because the addressable customer — someone dissatisfied enough with Chase or Bank of America to switch their primary account to an unproven foreign brand — turned out to be a thin slice of the market, and acquiring them unit-economically made no sense. Chime, which raised over $2 billion and had the advantage of being American, still hasn't turned a profit at scale doing roughly the same thing domestically. Competing for that customer with less brand recognition and a foreign headquarters is a worse version of a bad business.

Revolut's actual edge is behavioral, not promotional. The 50 million-plus users it has built globally are disproportionately people who live across borders — immigrants, frequent travelers, remote workers paid in one currency and spending in another, small business owners with international suppliers. This cohort uses Revolut not because it has a pretty app but because the alternative is paying 3 to 4 percent FX markups and waiting three to five days for wire transfers to clear. That pain is real and recurring, and no US incumbent has seriously attacked it because their core infrastructure — the correspondent banking network, the SWIFT messaging layer, the domestic ACH rails — was built for a different era and a different customer.

The stablecoin angle is where this gets structurally interesting. A national bank charter, if approved, would give Revolut the regulatory standing to issue dollar-denominated stablecoins under the framework being built around the GENIUS Act, a piece of US legislation moving through Congress that would create a formal licensing regime for stablecoin issuers. That matters because it means Revolut could settle cross-border transactions in tokenized dollars on its own rails rather than routing through correspondent banks and paying the margin that comes with it. The correspondent banking system is one of the least-discussed toll booths in global finance — banks charge each other for the privilege of moving money across borders, and those costs get passed downstream. A chartered stablecoin issuer with an existing user base on both sides of a transaction can theoretically compress that toll significantly. That is a margin story, not a growth-hacking story, and it is categorically different from what Monzo was selling.

The timing risk is real and worth taking seriously. Charter approvals from the Office of the Comptroller of the Currency routinely take 18 to 24 months from filing, and that timeline assumes no political disruption, no examiner requests for additional documentation, and no intervening policy shifts. The stablecoin regime Revolut is building toward does not yet fully exist — it is being written in real time on Capitol Hill. A different administration, or even a different Treasury Secretary, could reshape the supervisory environment in ways that delay or redefine the opportunity. Revolut is essentially making a $500 million options bet on two external variables — regulatory approval and legislative stability — neither of which it controls. That is the honest read of the risk profile, and allocators should size their expectations accordingly.

Tech & AI China Open Source Frontier Models

MiniMax is open-sourcing a 2.7-trillion-parameter model. The 'open' part is the con.

  • MiniMax's planned M3 Pro model has 2.7 trillion parameters — roughly six times its current flagship M3 at 428 billion — and would be the largest Chinese AI model released to date
  • A model at this scale costs an estimated $50–150 million to train even at Chinese cost structures, meaning open-sourcing it is a deliberate loss-leader strategy, not a charitable research contribution
  • Beijing is actively drafting export controls that could restrict which foreign developers and companies are allowed to use China's most capable AI models

The take

MiniMax, a Hong Kong-listed Chinese lab, plans to open-source a 2.7-trillion-parameter model — call it M3 Pro — landing as early as this quarter. That's roughly six times its current flagship and the largest Chinese model shipped to date. The consensus read is "another cheap open-weight squeeze on OpenAI and Anthropic margins." True, but that's last year's story. The new tell is the scale itself, and what it says about who is subsidizing whom.

Here's what's actually going on. Nobody open-sources a 2.7T-parameter model because it's cheap to train — that run costs somewhere in the $50-150M range even at Chinese cost structures. You do it because the weights are a loss leader for something else. For DeepSeek and Moonshot, that something is national strategic positioning. For MiniMax, a public company in a brutal home market where AI names have whipsawed all year, it's a share-price event dressed as a research release. The move isn't "we beat Anthropic on cost." It's "we're the last Chinese lab standing after the domestic bloodbath consolidates." The real competition here is DeepSeek versus Zhipu versus Moonshot versus MiniMax — four labs torching capital to be the one open-weight brand enterprises default to. Only one or two survive that.

The second-order effect nobody is pricing: Beijing is reportedly weighing export curbs on who abroad can use its best models. Sit with that. China spent two years flooding the world with free weights to hollow out the American pricing premium, and the moment it works, the state starts contemplating a valve on the pipe. That's not contradiction — it's the plan. Get global developers hooked on the free tier, establish the dependency, then decide the terms of access from a position of leverage. It's the Huawei playbook applied to intelligence. The "open" in open-weight is a customer-acquisition phase, not a permanent commitment.

For allocators, the mistake is treating this as a margin story about Western labs. The margin story is real but already in the price — everyone knows token pricing compresses toward zero for commodity workloads. The mispriced thing is sovereignty risk on the demand side. If your portfolio companies have built agentic stacks on Qwen or DeepSeek or, soon, MiniMax weights because the economics were irresistible, you've imported a supply-chain dependency that Beijing can throttle by decree. That's not a hypothetical — the curbs are being drafted now.

The clean trade isn't picking a lab. It's the arbitrage between "open" as a marketing state and "open" as a legal guarantee. The labs that win the next cycle aren't the ones with the biggest parameter count. They're the ones offering weights an enterprise can actually self-host and fine-tune with no jurisdiction able to switch them off. Count how few of those there are.

The other side

The straightforward counterargument is that open-weight models — models whose underlying code and trained weights are publicly released for anyone to download and run — genuinely democratize AI development regardless of the motivations behind the release. DeepSeek's R1 and Alibaba's Qwen models are already running in air-gapped enterprise environments and on-premise data centers worldwide, where Beijing has no practical ability to switch them off once deployed. If M3 Pro delivers frontier-grade performance at zero licensing cost, the geopolitical framing matters a lot less to a developer in São Paulo or Berlin than the benchmark scores do.

Say more →

Start with the domestic picture, because that context almost always gets skipped. MiniMax is a publicly traded company listed in Hong Kong, competing in a home market where four serious labs — DeepSeek, Zhipu AI, Moonshot AI, and MiniMax itself — are simultaneously burning capital to establish dominance. Each of those firms is releasing capable, cheap models in hopes of becoming the default open-weight brand that global enterprises eventually standardize on. The economics of that race are brutal: only one or two of them can plausibly survive it as independent businesses, and the window to pull ahead is narrow. M3 Pro is as much a statement to Hong Kong investors and Chinese enterprise buyers as it is a shot across OpenAI's bow. When AI stocks have swung violently all year, shipping the largest open model in Chinese history is the kind of headline that moves your multiple.

Zoom out and the Western labs' actual problem comes into focus. The margin compression on commodity AI tasks — things like summarization, classification, and routine code generation — is already visible and already acknowledged by investors. What is not yet priced is the dependency risk accumulating quietly on the demand side. Developers and enterprise engineering teams have spent the last eighteen months gravitating toward Chinese open-weight models because the economics were, frankly, irresistible. When a capable model is free to download and cheap to run, the rational short-term move is obvious. But that adoption curve now looks like a classic platform-capture pattern: lower the cost of switching to near zero, build habitual reliance, then introduce friction on your own terms once the installed base is large enough.

The Huawei analogy from the take deserves to be taken seriously. Huawei spent years becoming structurally embedded in global telecom infrastructure — not through coercion but through competitive pricing and genuine engineering quality — and that embeddedness gave Beijing leverage that neither Huawei nor its customers fully appreciated until the rules changed. The intelligence-layer version of that dynamic is more direct, because software dependencies are easier to toggle remotely than physical hardware. Export controls on AI model access would not need to be technically sophisticated to cause real disruption; they just need to exist, and the policy process to create them is already underway.

The parameter count itself is worth a beat of skepticism. Larger models do tend to handle complex, multi-step reasoning better than smaller ones, but the relationship is not linear, and the gap between a well-trained 400-billion-parameter model and a 2.7-trillion-parameter model is not six times the capability. Architecture decisions, training data quality, and inference efficiency all matter as much as raw scale. MiniMax's current flagship has not yet demonstrated that it competes at the true frontier on reasoning benchmarks, and a model six times bigger trained by the same team on a similar timeline is not automatically a leap forward. The weights dropping is the moment of truth, and that moment has not arrived yet.

The durable investment question this raises is not which lab has the biggest model. It is which open-weight models carry a genuine legal and jurisdictional guarantee that no government can revoke access after deployment. That category is considerably smaller than the list of models currently described as open-source, and the gap between those two lists is exactly where the real enterprise risk lives.

The Fed has no center of gravity. Warsh's silence is the loudest signal in the room.

  • The June FOMC minutes ran only 14 pages — deliberately shorter under new Fed Chair Kevin Warsh, who is explicitly pulling back on the kind of forward guidance that tells markets where rates are headed next
  • Among the 18 policymakers who submitted forecasts, exactly half supported raising rates by year-end and half supported holding or cutting — a genuine nine-to-nine deadlock, not a close call with a clear lean
  • Core PCE inflation — the Fed's preferred measure, which strips out food and energy — has already broken above 3%, and the Fed's internal research team raised its inflation forecasts for both this year and next

The take

The June FOMC minutes, released July 8, are the first under Kevin Warsh's chairmanship, and they're notable for something the market is under-reading: the committee is now genuinely split, nine to nine, on whether the next move is a hike or a cut. Warsh himself declined to submit a rate forecast, which everyone is reading as a philosophical statement about forward guidance. It's actually a tell about the substance. He can't submit a dot because there is no median dot worth defending. The Fed has lost its center of gravity.

Here's what nobody is pricing. The consensus read — Goldman, Morgan Stanley, Citi all singing the same "data-dependent" hymn — treats this as a normal cyclical inflation question that resolves when core PCE drifts from 3.4% back toward 3.0%. That framing assumes AI infrastructure inflation is transitory. It isn't. When "many" officials flag that data centers are bidding up semiconductors, industrial equipment, and — critically — electricity, they are describing a structural demand shock, not a supply-chain hangover. Apple raising MacBook and iPad prices on memory costs isn't a headline curiosity; it's the first visible pass-through of a capex supercycle into consumer prices. Hyperscaler capex is running north of $500B annually. That money doesn't ask what the CPI print was last month.

The deeper problem is that AI is simultaneously deflationary in labor and inflationary in inputs, and the Fed has no model that resolves the two. Every prior tech buildout — fiber, cloud — was net disinflationary because the marginal cost of the output collapsed. This one collapses cognitive-labor cost while creating a hard, physical bottleneck in power generation and grid capacity that takes a decade to build out. You cannot cut rates into an electricity shortage and you cannot hike your way out of a productivity boom. That's the actual reason Warsh won't commit to a path. The old reaction function is broken and he knows it.

For allocators, the trade isn't guessing whether October delivers a cut. It's positioning for a Fed that stays stuck at 3.5–3.75% far longer than the curve implies, while structurally higher input inflation grinds against structurally lower labor inflation. That regime crushes the duration-and-growth barbell everyone piled into. It favors the physical layer of the AI stack — power generation, transmission, grid equipment, the utilities with pricing power — over the software names that assume infinite cheap compute. The market is trading this as a rates story. It's an electricity story wearing a rates costume, and the Fed just admitted it in fourteen pages.

The other side

The bull case for a soft landing hasn't actually broken down. Goldman Sachs is projecting core PCE falling from 3.4% to 3.0% by year-end, and core CPI dropping from 2.9% to 2.6%, with month-over-month readings staying mild in the interim. If those numbers arrive on schedule, the committee's hawks lose their argument and Citi's baseline — two 25-basis-point cuts before year-end, starting in October — becomes not just plausible but likely. The deadlock looks different if you believe, as many committee members evidently do, that AI-driven and tariff-driven inflation pressures are genuinely temporary disturbances that dissipate without requiring tightening. Morgan Stanley's Michael Gapen also makes the point that a 'minority' flagging a theoretical case for hiking is not the same as a majority pushing for one — nobody actually wanted to press the button in June.

Say more →

Start with the institutional context. Kevin Warsh, who took over as Fed chair earlier this year, is a known skeptic of the forward guidance model that defined the Bernanke-through-Powell era — the practice of publishing dot plots and explicit rate projections to anchor market expectations. The decision to shrink the minutes to 14 pages and decline to submit his own rate forecast isn't just a stylistic preference. It reflects a view that heavily telegraphed policy paths create their own rigidities, forcing the committee to defend projections rather than respond to data. That philosophy might be correct, but its immediate effect is to strip the market of the anchor it has relied on for over a decade. When the chair won't tell you where he thinks rates are going, it isn't a negotiating posture — it's an admission that he genuinely doesn't know.

The Wall Street commentary that followed the minutes release is worth examining for what it reveals about the consensus blindspot. Goldman, Morgan Stanley, and Citi all converged on the same framing: the Fed is data-dependent, inflation will likely drift lower, and the next meaningful move is probably a cut sometime in 2027 or possibly late 2026. That framing is internally consistent if you believe the AI infrastructure buildout is behaving like previous technology cycles. The fiber optic boom of the late 1990s and the cloud expansion of the 2010s both created short-term demand surges in specialized equipment, followed by a deflationary collapse in the cost of the output they produced. Bandwidth got cheap. Storage got cheap. The marginal cost of deploying software fell toward zero.

The current cycle is partly following that script — AI is compressing the cost of knowledge work at a pace that has no real precedent — but it diverges in one critical physical dimension. Fiber and cloud infrastructure required capital but not sustained, enormous draws on the physical power grid. Data centers running large AI models do. Hyperscaler capital expenditure — the combined annual spending by companies like Microsoft, Google, Amazon, and Meta on AI infrastructure — is now running above $500 billion per year globally and accelerating. That spending translates directly into competition for electricity generation, high-voltage transformers, specialized semiconductors, and industrial cooling equipment. These are not categories where supply responds quickly to price signals. A new power plant takes seven to ten years to permit and build. Grid-scale transformer lead times have stretched past two years in some markets. You cannot manufacture your way out of a decade-long underinvestment in generation capacity in eighteen months because the Fed held rates steady.

Apple raising prices on MacBooks and iPads because memory chip costs are rising is the consumer-facing tip of this dynamic. The pass-through from hyperscaler demand into component pricing into end-consumer goods is a mechanism the Fed's standard models weren't built to capture, because no prior technology cycle produced it at this scale.

The deeper policy trap is the one flagged in the take: AI is simultaneously pushing labor costs down and physical input costs up, and those two forces don't cancel neatly. Prior disinflationary tech cycles worked in part because they didn't generate offsetting physical bottlenecks. This one does. A Fed that cuts into genuine power-generation scarcity risks re-accelerating the input-price spiral. A Fed that hikes to suppress AI-driven capex risks strangling a productivity transformation that could ultimately be the most powerful disinflationary force in a generation. Neither lever fits the problem cleanly, which is exactly why nine officials are pointing one direction and nine are pointing the other.

For investors, the practical implication is that the 3.5–3.75% range is stickier than the rates market currently prices. The physical infrastructure layer of the AI buildout — power generation, transmission equipment, grid operators, the subset of utilities with genuine pricing power — is priced for a world where the bottleneck gets resolved quickly. It probably doesn't.

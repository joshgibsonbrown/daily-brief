ICONIQ Portfolio Companies Databricks Enterprise AI Valuations

Databricks tripled its valuation in nineteen months. The round math and the business math are diverging.

  • Databricks hit a $188B valuation Thursday in a Coatue-led round — up from $134B in February and $62B in December 2024, across four fundraises in under two years
  • CEO Ali Ghodsi published internal benchmarks the same week showing his 3,000 engineers get better cost-adjusted coding results from Z.ai's GLM 5.2, a Chinese open-weight model, than from Anthropic or OpenAI's paid offerings
  • The round size is roughly $3B per other outlets, but Databricks declined to confirm it and hasn't received the cash yet — closing is expected later this summer

The take

Databricks announced a Coatue-led round Thursday at $188 billion, up from $134 billion in February and $62 billion in December 2024 — a tripling of valuation in nineteen months, across four rounds, without an IPO. The consensus read is that Databricks is the cleanest AI infrastructure bet in private markets: it sits on the enterprise data, it added the AI layer, and money is falling on it faster than it can spell out new share classes. That story is mostly true, which is exactly why it's mispriced.

Here's what nobody is pricing. The same week Databricks took a $54 billion valuation step-up on the AI narrative, its own CEO Ali Ghodsi published benchmarks showing that his 3,000 engineers get their best cost-adjusted results running Z.ai's GLM 5.2 — a Chinese open-weight model — over Anthropic and OpenAI's proprietary frontier models. Read that again. The company being valued on the AI premium is publicly documenting that the AI premium doesn't survive contact with an actual procurement spreadsheet. Databricks is short the very thing its multiple is long.

That's not a contradiction — it's the whole thesis, and it's better than the bulls understand. Databricks isn't valued as a model. It's valued as the toll booth that sits between enterprise data and whatever model wins. If frontier model prices collapse toward the GLM 5.2 floor, Databricks captures the surplus, because the governance, the data gravity, and now the harness layer — the agentic tooling that wraps the model — are where the switching costs actually live. Ghodsi telling the world that harness choice matters as much as model choice isn't candor. It's a land grab dressed as a benchmark blog post.

The read-across for allocators holding this through ICONIQ VII: you are not underwriting an AI lab, and you should stop letting the round narrative tell you that you are. You're underwriting a data-plumbing monopoly that benefits structurally from AI becoming a commoditized, price-competitive input. The risk isn't that GLM 5.2 wins. The risk is the opposite — that a proprietary lab builds enough of its own governance and orchestration stack to disintermediate the toll booth. Watch what OpenAI and Anthropic do at the harness and enterprise-data layer, not what they charge per token.

And be honest about the round mechanics. Databricks announced a valuation it doesn't yet have the cash for, closing "later this summer," at a size it declined to disclose. When a private company front-runs its own markup because demand is so oversubscribed it sees no reason to stay quiet, that's a company managing its narrative as an asset. The $188 billion is real. The question is whether it's a business valuation or a fundraising valuation. At this cadence — a new round every five months — those have quietly become the same thing.

The other side

The toll-booth framing only holds if enterprises keep choosing Databricks as the neutral layer, and that neutrality is increasingly contested. Microsoft Fabric and Snowflake — two well-capitalized competitors — are both pushing integrated data-plus-AI stacks that bundle governance and orchestration with their own compute, removing the need for a separate infrastructure vendor. If two or three hyperscalers succeed in making their platforms the default place enterprise data lives, Databricks' data gravity flips from a moat into a migration problem, and a $188 billion valuation built on switching-cost logic gets painful very fast.

The valuation trajectory here is worth sitting with for a moment. Databricks went from $62 billion to $188 billion in nineteen months without going public, without reporting audited revenue growth, and without any single product launch that fundamentally changed its market position. What changed is the narrative environment. Enterprise AI became the most legible growth story available to late-stage private capital, and Databricks — founded in 2013 as a big-data analytics company, not an AI lab — was positioned closely enough to benefit from the re-rating. That's not an accusation. It's how private market cycles work. The risk is that allocators confuse proximity to a trend with ownership of it.

The benchmarking episode Ghodsi published last week is more revealing than it might look on first read. Databricks tested AI models against the actual coding tasks its own engineers perform, found that GLM 5.2 — the open-weight model from Z.ai, a Chinese AI company — matched or beat proprietary models from Anthropic and OpenAI on the hardest task categories, and did so at lower total cost. Then Ghodsi added the part that really matters for Databricks' business: the agentic harness — the software layer that manages context and instructions around the model, tools like Codex or Claude Code — affected cost and quality as much as the model itself. Open-source harness Pi came out favorably. The blog post was framed as vendor-neutral cost advice. It was also a clear signal about where Databricks wants to compete.

If model prices converge toward the GLM 5.2 floor — and the direction of travel on frontier model pricing over the last eighteen months suggests they will — the value in an AI workflow shifts upstream toward data and downstream toward orchestration. Databricks already owns the data layer for a significant slice of Fortune 500 companies. The harness and orchestration layer is the logical adjacency, and Ghodsi naming it publicly as a cost lever is the company staking a claim before the market fully prices that layer.

The historical parallel that comes to mind is Oracle in the late 1990s. Oracle didn't build the internet. It built the database infrastructure that sat underneath everything the internet ran on, and it extracted durable margin from that position for decades even as the application layer above it commoditized repeatedly. Databricks is trying to occupy an analogous position in enterprise AI: not the model, not the application, but the plumbing that everything else depends on. That's a legitimate and potentially very durable business. It also has a ceiling that the current multiple may not reflect.

The real threat to this thesis is not Chinese open-weight models. It is vertical integration from above. If OpenAI or Anthropic decide that owning enterprise governance, data connectivity, and orchestration tooling is necessary to defend their customer relationships — and the economics of frontier model pricing increasingly push them in that direction — then the toll booth gets bypassed, not underpriced. Neither company has fully cracked enterprise data management yet, but both have the distribution and the balance sheets to try. That is the scenario worth stress-testing, not whether GLM 5.2 keeps winning coding benchmarks.

Washington just became the distribution layer for American AI. The labs don't get a vote.

  • The White House blocked Anthropic's Claude Mythos 5 and Fable 5 last month over national security concerns, restoring access only after weeks of negotiation — the first known use of a de facto government veto on a frontier AI release.
  • Gold Eagle, a new White House cyber-vulnerability clearinghouse launched this week, will put the administration in charge of approving partner lists for Anthropic's and OpenAI's most capable models going forward.
  • Moonshot AI's Kimi K3 — a Chinese frontier model — launched the same day as Gold Eagle and matched or exceeded OpenAI's GPT-5.6 and Anthropic's Fable 5 on at least one independent benchmark.

The take

The White House quietly took control of who gets to use the most capable AI models in America this week. Through a new cyber-vulnerability clearinghouse called Gold Eagle, the administration will now approve the partner lists for Anthropic's and OpenAI's frontier models — the decision that used to sit with the labs themselves. The tell that this is real and not a press-release gesture: last month the government blocked Anthropic's Claude Mythos 5 and Fable 5 outright over national security concerns, and only restored access after weeks of negotiation. That's not advisory. That's a kill switch, already tested in production.

The official line — "we don't provide approvals for AI releases," participation is "voluntary" — is the part you should ignore entirely. Every gating regime in history was voluntary until it wasn't, and the operational reality here is that two of the three most valuable AI companies on earth can no longer ship their best product to a customer without Washington signing off on the customer. That is distribution authority. Acquired without legislation, without an agency, without a Federal Register notice anyone can sue over. When the government controls who your model can be sold to, it controls your revenue curve, and it did so this week for free.

Here's what nobody is pricing. This permanently changes the risk profile of frontier-lab equity, and it changes it in favor of the incumbents even as it caps their upside. OpenAI and Anthropic just became quasi-regulated utilities with a single, capricious counterparty who can freeze their release calendar for "weeks of negotiation" at any moment. That's terrible for a growth multiple and wonderful for a moat — a new entrant now has to clear the same political gate with none of the relationships. If you hold exposure to these names through the secondary market, you are now underwriting political risk you cannot hedge, priced as if it were pure technology risk. It isn't.

The timing makes the whole thing look ridiculous, which is the second-order signal. Moonshot's Kimi K3 shipped the same day and matched or beat Fable and GPT-5.6 on at least one independent benchmark. David Sacks, until recently the administration's own AI czar, called it "concerning" and said plainly: this is how you lose the race. He's right on the mechanism. Export controls and access-gating assume you're the only one who can build the thing. That assumption expired sometime in 2024. Every week the US spends approving partner lists, the marginal buyer's alternative gets cheaper and better and answers to Beijing's rules, not Washington's.

The allocation read: the American frontier-lab premium is now partly a sovereignty tax, and the durable capital is quietly rotating toward open-weight and self-hostable stacks that no government can gate.

The other side

The strongest case for this regime is that the threat is genuinely asymmetric: a single bad actor with access to a state-of-the-art cybersecurity AI model could compromise critical infrastructure in ways that dwarf any commercial downside from slower rollouts. Anthropic's Mythos and OpenAI's comparable cyber models are categorically different from a consumer chatbot — they were purpose-built to find and exploit vulnerabilities at machine speed. If a foreign intelligence service, a ransomware syndicate, or even a reckless domestic operator got early access, the harm could be irreversible. The argument isn't that the labs are reckless; it's that national-security vetting is simply not a competency any private company has earned the right to waive unilaterally.

The mechanism worth understanding is how quietly this happened. There was no congressional vote, no new agency created, no public comment period. The legal scaffolding is a June executive order asking labs to give the government early model access for testing — framed, pointedly, as voluntary. Gold Eagle then operationalizes that ask into something functionally indistinguishable from a licensing regime. The White House gets early access, reviews the partner list, and the historical record now shows it will block a release if it objects. Call that voluntary if you like. The labs apparently did not.

The utility parallel in the take is the right frame, and it is worth extending. When an industry gets quasi-regulated through informal pressure rather than statute, the incumbents almost always end up preferring it that way after the initial shock. A formal regulatory process is at least contestable in court. This arrangement is not — there is no Federal Register notice to challenge, no defined standard to meet, no appeals process. What there is, is a relationship. And relationships favor the players who already have them. OpenAI and Anthropic have been cultivating Washington ties for two years; a new entrant trying to commercialize a frontier model in 2025 now inherits this gating requirement with none of that accumulated goodwill. The moat just got deeper and more political at the same time.

The equity implications are real and still being underestimated. Secondary-market positions in Anthropic and OpenAI have been priced primarily on capability trajectory and revenue growth — the standard framework for pre-IPO technology companies. That framework does not have a good input for 'government can freeze your release calendar for an indeterminate period at any moment for reasons it will not fully disclose.' Growth multiples are built on predictable compounding. Unpredictable political intervention is the enemy of that math. The irony is that the same dynamic that compresses the upside also reinforces the moat, so the valuation question is genuinely hard — you are now holding something closer to a regulated infrastructure asset than a pure-growth software company, and the market has not repriced that yet.

The Chinese capability convergence makes the timing genuinely painful. Export controls and access-gating are coherent strategies when you hold a commanding lead — you are rationing something scarce. They become costly when the gap closes, because you are then rationing something that a competitor is giving away. Kimi K3 landing on the same day as Gold Eagle is not a coincidence in a causal sense, but it is a brutal juxtaposition in a strategic one. Every governance cycle the US adds to its frontier-model release process is another week a capable open-weight alternative — one that answers to no partner-list approval — gets cheaper, more capable, and more attractive to exactly the international buyers the US would prefer to keep in its orbit. The sovereignty tax is real, and the buyers who can avoid paying it are increasingly able to do so.

Private Markets Secondaries LP Conflicts Carlyle

The exit door in private equity quietly moved. Most LPs are still standing at the old one.

  • AlpInvest's AAF II closed at a $1.7B hard cap on July 15, 2026 — a 393% increase from its 2023 predecessor fund, which closed at a fraction of that size
  • Continuation vehicles now represent about 15% of total sponsor-backed exit value, up from 8% in 2021, and account for roughly 89% of all GP-led secondary market volume
  • Global GP-led secondary volume hit approximately $106–115 billion in 2025, with the number of continuation vehicle deals over $1 billion jumping 57% year over year

The take

AlpInvest, Carlyle's $107 billion secondaries arm, just closed a fund called AAF II at a $1.7 billion hard cap — nearly 4x the size of the version it raised only three years ago — dedicated entirely to buying into single-asset continuation vehicles, the deals where a private equity firm parks its best portfolio company in a new fund instead of selling it. The reason this specific fundraise is worth your attention isn't the size. It's that a 393% jump in three years is a market telling you the exit door has quietly moved, and most LPs are still standing at the old one.

Here's what's actually going on. Continuation vehicles now account for roughly 89% of all GP-led secondary volume and about 15% of total sponsor-backed exit value, up from 8% in 2021. Coller Capital said the quiet part out loud: whether the M&A and IPO markets reopen in 2026 "doesn't really matter" to continuation vehicle demand. Read that again. The consensus story is that continuation funds are a liquidity patch for a frozen exit environment — a bridge until normal M&A returns. Coller just told you it isn't a bridge. It's the destination. GPs have discovered they can hold their winners indefinitely, keep charging fees and carry, and never subject the mark to an actual arm's-length buyer. Why would they ever go back?

That's the second-order effect nobody is pricing. The DPI problem that's strangling PE fundraising — LPs not getting cash back — is precisely what's fueling this. A continuation vehicle manufactures a "distribution" event without a real sale. The GP sets the price, the GP manages the asset before and after, and the GP resets its own carry clock on its best company. ILPA has been screaming about this since 2023 because "conflicts are inherent as GPs sit on both sides of the deal." That is not a bug the industry is working out. It's the product. AlpInvest is raising 4x the capital to be the counterparty that makes the GP's self-dealing look validated by an independent buyer.

So think about what AlpInvest actually is in this trade. Perriello's team has done 31 of these since 2018 and wants to do many more. A specialist repeat buyer whose franchise depends on GPs bringing it deal flow is structurally disincentivized from pricing aggressively against those same GPs. That's the tell in "25 years of GP relationships" — the relationship is the asset, and the relationship prices the trade.

The allocator read: when a GP offers to roll you into its trophy holding at a "negotiated" price, understand you're not being offered liquidity — you're being offered the chance to finance the GP's fee extension. Take the cash. Let AlpInvest own the conflict.

The other side

The bull case for continuation vehicles as a genuine LP-friendly tool is real and shouldn't be dismissed. When a GP has a genuinely high-performing company stuck in a fund approaching its legal end-of-life, a continuation vehicle can be the only mechanism that lets existing LPs participate in remaining upside rather than forcing a distressed sale at the wrong moment in the cycle. AlpInvest and peers like Coller Capital and Lexington Partners bring genuine price discipline and underwriting rigor — 31 transactions and roughly $7 billion committed is not a passive rubber stamp operation — and a well-run continuation vehicle process with independent valuation and LP advisory committee oversight can legitimately replicate the governance protections of a third-party sale. If M&A and IPO markets stay suppressed for another two or three years, the alternative to continuation vehicles isn't a clean exit; it's a forced sale at a worse price.

To understand why AlpInvest's fundraise is a signal and not just a press release, it helps to understand what changed structurally in the private equity exit market starting around 2022. The standard PE playbook assumed a roughly four-to-six year hold, then a sale — either to a strategic acquirer, another buyout firm, or through an IPO. All three of those channels seized up more or less simultaneously when interest rates rose sharply and public market valuations compressed. Buyout firms that had raised enormous funds in 2019 and 2020 found themselves sitting on mature holdings they couldn't move. The continuation vehicle was already an established niche tool at that point, but necessity turned it into infrastructure.

The speed of that transition is visible in the data. Average continuation vehicle size rose roughly 15% year over year to about $1 billion in 2025. Eighty-three percent of the top 100 global buyout sponsors have now used the structure at least once. Three years ago that number was a fraction of that. What was exotic capital markets plumbing is now standard operating procedure at every major private equity firm on the planet.

What's less discussed is what this does to the LP experience of private equity as an asset class. The traditional bargain was straightforward: you commit capital for ten to twelve years, the GP invests it, sells the companies, and returns cash. DPI — distributions to paid-in capital, meaning the actual cash returned relative to what you invested — was the ultimate scorecard. A GP sitting on a great paper return but returning no cash was failing at the core job. Continuation vehicles complicate that bargain in a specific way. When a GP moves its best company into a new vehicle at a price it largely controls, the old fund's LP can either take cash out or roll forward. Rolling forward sounds attractive — you get to stay in the winner — but what you're actually doing is agreeing to extend the GP's management fees and carried interest on that asset for another several years, at terms set in the new vehicle, at a price the GP had significant influence over setting.

ILPA, the trade body for institutional investors in private funds, published formal best-practice guidance on continuation vehicles in 2023 precisely because the conflict structure is so embedded. The GP negotiating the asset transfer is the same party who will manage it afterward and benefit from the carry reset. ILPA has since moved to update that guidance in 2026 as volume has scaled, a sign that the original framework wasn't containing the problem. The fact that regulators and LP advocates are on their second iteration of guidance within three years tells you the governance gap is widening faster than the rules meant to close it.

AlpInvest's specific positioning in this market deserves scrutiny on its own terms. The firm describes its competitive advantage as 25 years of GP relationships and proprietary data across tens of thousands of private companies. That is genuinely useful for underwriting — knowing a GP's track record across dozens of prior deals is real information. But a repeat counterparty whose continued deal flow depends on maintaining those GP relationships faces a structural pressure toward accommodating pricing. The firm that pushes too hard on valuation gets fewer calls next time. That's not a character flaw; it's the math of a franchise business. It's also why describing AlpInvest as an independent price validator in a continuation vehicle transaction deserves at least a raised eyebrow.

The broader second-order implication is for what PE actually delivers as an asset class going forward. If the best assets never reach arm's-length buyers — cycling instead from one fund vehicle to the next under the same GP — then the return series LPs use to evaluate managers becomes harder to audit. Marks stay clean, carry gets harvested, fees continue, and the moment of true price discovery keeps getting deferred. That's fine in a rising market. In a flat or falling one, it's how a lot of unrealized losses stay unrealized for a very long time.

Family Office & Wealth Planning Estate Tax Tax Policy Philanthropy

The estate-tax sunset is dead. The deadline-arbitrage business died with it.

  • Congress set the federal estate-tax exemption at $15 million per person ($30 million per married couple) starting in 2026, indexed to inflation, with no expiration date — the first permanent high exemption since the 2017 tax law created a temporary one
  • The prior exemption was set to snap back to roughly $7 million per person after 2025, a cliff that had driven a decade of rushed irrevocable trust formations, last-minute gifting strategies, and valuation work for closely held businesses
  • The generation-skipping transfer (GST) exemption — which allows wealth to pass to grandchildren and beyond without triggering an extra layer of federal tax at each generation — is now also locked at $15 million, directly enabling multigenerational dynasty trust strategies that previously carried legislative risk

The take

Congress just made the estate-tax exemption permanent at $15 million per person — $30 million for a couple — starting in 2026, and it indexed the number to inflation. The headline reads like a gift to the wealthy, and it is. But the more interesting fact is what got killed: the sunset. For a decade, the estate-planning industry ran on the fear of reversion. That fear is now gone, and a lot of people whose business model depended on it are about to find out they were selling insurance against a fire that Congress just extinguished.

Here's what nobody is pricing in. The urgency trade is dead. The entire post-2017 boom in irrevocable trust formation — the spousal lifetime access trusts, the "use it or lose it" gifting sprints, the year-end scrambles that generated billions in legal and valuation fees — was manufactured by the 2025 cliff. Advisers weren't selling estate planning; they were selling deadline arbitrage. With no deadline, the rational move for a family under $30 million is to do nothing, keep the step-up in cost basis at death, and preserve optionality. That's a structural revenue cut for the trust-and-estates bar and for the appraisal shops that made a living discounting closely held LLC interests under time pressure.

The second-order effect lands on philanthropy, and this is where allocators should pay attention. The source buries it, but the implication is enormous: when the exemption was $5 million, the marginal charitable bequest bought you a 40% tax offset. At $30 million per couple, the vast majority of estates that used to face federal tax now pass clean. The tax-driven donor is being removed from the market. What's left is legacy-and-governance-driven giving — smaller in aggregate, lumpier, and far more concentrated in the truly nine-figure households where the exemption is a rounding error. If you run a family foundation or advise on DAF flows, the mid-tier donor pipeline just thinned.

For the genuinely UHNW — the clients your firm actually serves — the exemption is almost irrelevant. Someone with a $500 million estate shelters 6% of it and pays 40% on the rest whether the number is $12 million or $15 million. For them the game was never the exemption; it was GRATs, freeze partnerships, and offshore structuring, none of which this law touches. So the permanence is a middle-class-millionaire event dressed up as a UHNW story.

The one thing to watch is the word "permanent." It survives exactly as long as the next Congress wants it to, and a $37 trillion debt stack makes transfer taxes an obvious future target. The smart structuring now isn't to gift aggressively — it's to build decanting and power-of-appointment flexibility into every trust so you can respond when the number moves. Permanence is a marketing term. Optionality is the actual asset.

The other side

The case for aggressive planning hasn't actually gone away — it's just shifted. A $37 trillion federal debt load makes transfer taxes one of the most politically accessible revenue levers a future Congress could pull, and estate-tax exemptions have swung from $1 million to $15 million and back within living memory. Families who interpret 'permanent' as genuinely permanent and stand down on trust funding could find themselves caught flat-footed if the exemption drops to $5 million in 2031 the same way it was always threatening to drop in 2026. The optionality argument cuts both ways: the cost of pre-funding an irrevocable trust today, when the exemption is high, is low compared to the cost of trying to do it in a compressed window under a hostile future tax regime.

Say more →

Start with the business model disruption, because that is the most immediate and least-discussed consequence. The trust-and-estates legal industry had a reliable forcing function for roughly eight years: a statutory deadline that made inaction feel reckless. Law firms, valuation shops, and family office advisers built recurring revenue around the annual rhythm of families asking whether this was finally the year to move assets into irrevocable structures before the window closed. That rhythm is gone. For families whose combined estate sits below $30 million, the actuarially correct answer is now to hold, preserve the step-up in cost basis at death — meaning heirs inherit assets valued at the date-of-death price, wiping out embedded capital gains — and revisit only if the law changes. That is a genuinely different planning posture than the industry has operated under for a decade, and fee income will reflect it.

The charitable giving implication deserves more weight than the coverage has given it. Donor-advised funds (DAFs) — vehicles where donors get an immediate tax deduction and distribute grants to charities over time — and private family foundations have grown substantially in part because estate-tax exposure gave wealthy families a hard financial reason to redirect assets toward philanthropy rather than let the government claim 40 cents on the dollar. That math changes materially when the taxable estate shrinks or disappears. A household with a $20 million estate no longer faces federal estate tax under the new rules, which means the philanthropic bequest that once offset a real liability is now purely an expression of preference. Preferences are real, but they are stickier and harder to monetize than tax savings. Fundraisers and foundation consultants who relied on the tax argument as a door-opener into mid-tier wealthy households are going to need a different pitch.

The historical parallel worth holding in mind is the 2001-to-2010 arc. The Economic Growth and Tax Relief Reconciliation Act of 2001 phased the exemption up and the rate down, and then — in one of the stranger moments in tax history — repealed the estate tax entirely for a single year in 2010 before it was set to snap back to 2001 levels in 2011. The uncertainty that followed that cycle produced exactly the kind of planning whiplash that Congress has now, at least structurally, tried to resolve. But the resolution in 2010 required the Tax Relief Act to be passed in a lame-duck session, at the last possible moment, under enormous political pressure. The lesson is that 'permanent' is a term of art in tax legislation, not a constitutional guarantee.

For the segment of the wealth management industry that actually serves nine-figure and ten-figure clients, the relevant planning tools remain largely untouched by this law. Grantor retained annuity trusts (GRATs), which allow appreciation on assets to pass to heirs with minimal gift-tax cost, and installment sales to intentionally defective grantor trusts (a structure that removes appreciation from a taxable estate while the grantor continues paying income tax on trust earnings) are still the primary levers at that level. The $15 million exemption barely registers when the estate is $200 million. What registers is the spread between the IRS's assumed growth rate and the actual return on transferred assets, and that spread is driven by portfolio construction and deal access, not by the exemption level. The permanence story, in other words, is genuinely most relevant to the $15 to $60 million household — the upper-middle of the wealth distribution — and that cohort is precisely the one where advisers need to recalibrate their value proposition now that deadline urgency is no longer the product.

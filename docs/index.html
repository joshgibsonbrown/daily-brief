GP financing is the best trade in private equity. Everyone else is still looking at the underlying deals.

  • Arctos — a firm that takes equity stakes in and provides financing to other alternative asset managers, recently agreed to be acquired by KKR — closed a record $6.2 billion fund focused entirely on lending to and buying stakes in other funds, not in operating companies.
  • The fund is the largest ever raised for GP solutions, a category that provides liquidity to fund managers who need cash to seed new funds, honor their own capital commitments, or buy out retiring partners.
  • The close comes amid a 22-month average fundraising cycle for mid-market private equity managers — meaning the pool of stressed borrowers Arctos is targeting has rarely been larger.

The take

Arctos, the sports-and-GP-stakes shop that KKR agreed to acquire earlier this year, just closed a record $6.2 billion fund not to buy companies but to lend and inject equity into other alternative asset managers. That's the tell worth sitting with: the biggest new pool of capital in this corner of private markets exists to finance the financiers. When the smartest money in the room stops buying assets and starts underwriting the people who buy assets, you're being told something about where the returns actually are.

Here's what's actually going on. The GP stakes and GP financing business is a bet that the management-fee-and-carry machine is more durable than the underlying deals it makes. And that bet is correct. A buyout fund can miss its return target and still pay its GP handsomely for a decade through fees on committed capital. Arctos isn't taking asset risk — it's taking manager-survival risk, which is a dramatically better place to sit when exits are frozen and DPI is scarce. You're clipping a coupon off the one part of the private equity income statement that doesn't depend on the IPO window reopening.

Now connect it to the KKR deal. Marc Rowan told everyone that pure buyout is a great business but not a growth business, and the big four are quietly rebuilding as fee-gathering platforms. KKR buying Arctos isn't diversification — it's KKR positioning to finance the mid-market GPs who can't raise Fund V on their own, and to collect a permanent tax on the fragmentation of the industry below them. The $6.2 billion is dry powder aimed squarely at the 22-month-fundraise, DPI-starved managers described in every LP letter. Those GPs need liquidity to seed the next fund, fund their own commitments, and buy out retiring partners. Arctos is the payday lender to a stressed industry, and it raised a record fund because the borrowers are lining up.

The second-order effect nobody is pricing: this is how the barbell gets locked in permanently. GP financing lets the survivors stay solvent long enough to consolidate, while the capital that funds their survival extracts equity and preferred returns on the way. It's not a rescue — it's a slow-motion recapitalization of the middle of the industry on terms set by the people at the top. For allocators, the read-across is uncomfortable. If your mid-market GP is taking Arctos-style financing, that's not a growth signal, it's a distress signal dressed as ambition, and someone senior is quietly getting bought out with capital that now sits ahead of your LP interest in the waterfall.

The best trade in private equity right now isn't a fund. It's owning the toll booth on everyone else's inability to raise one.

The other side

The GP financing model is only as durable as the management fees it clips, and those fees are not untouchable forever. Institutional LPs — the pension funds and endowments that fund the whole stack — are already pushing back on fee structures and culling their manager rosters, which means the mid-market GPs Arctos is financing may face structural revenue compression, not just a temporary liquidity squeeze. If enough of those managers fail to raise their next fund, Arctos ends up holding equity stakes in shrinking businesses, and a $6.2 billion fund built on manager-survival risk starts looking a lot more like manager-default risk.

The GP solutions category — firms that provide capital directly to fund managers rather than to the companies those managers invest in — has existed at the margins of private markets for roughly a decade, but it has accelerated sharply as the exit environment deteriorated. When IPO windows close and M&A slows, buyout funds stop returning cash to investors. Managers who promised DPI, meaning actual distributions of realized cash rather than paper gains, find themselves in an awkward position: they need to raise Fund V before Fund IV has proven anything. That is the specific gap Arctos is financing, and it is not a small gap right now.

Consider the math a mid-market GP actually faces. A firm managing two billion dollars in assets charges roughly two percent in annual management fees, producing forty million dollars in revenue before carry. That sounds comfortable until you account for the GP commit — the amount the firm itself must invest in its own new fund, typically one to three percent of fund size — partner buyouts as the founding generation retires, and the cost of running a competitive fundraise that now stretches almost two years. These are not trivial cash demands for firms that haven't seen a meaningful carry distribution in three or four years. Arctos is writing checks into exactly that gap, and in exchange it is taking preferred equity, royalty-like fee participation, or outright ownership stakes at prices set during a period of maximum GP desperation.

The KKR acquisition context matters here more than most coverage has acknowledged. KKR spent years articulating what its executives call the shift from a capital-light GP model to a permanent capital, fee-based platform. Buying Arctos is not a strategic experiment — it is a direct investment in the infrastructure that will let KKR sit one layer above the fragmented middle of the industry and collect structured returns as consolidation plays out below. The mid-market managers who borrow from Arctos today are, in many cases, pre-softening for eventual acquisition or merger. Capital that enters as a loan with warrants has a way of converting into influence over succession and strategy.

For limited partners — the institutional investors who allocate to these funds — the read-across is genuinely important and underappreciated. GP financing is senior to LP interests in the economics of a fund. When a manager takes on Arctos-style capital, that capital typically sits ahead of LPs in the fee waterfall and sometimes carries covenants that affect how the GP can operate the fund. LPs who don't scrutinize the capitalization of their GPs are accepting subordinated exposure without necessarily knowing it. The due diligence question that should now be standard — has your GP taken any third-party financing against management company cash flows or equity — is not yet standard. It should be.

Zooming out, what Arctos and its peers are building is a shadow banking layer for the private markets industry itself. The same dynamic that made specialty finance attractive after the 2008 banking retrenchment is now playing out inside alternatives. When traditional sources of GP liquidity dry up — strong carry distributions, easy fundraising, willing strategic acquirers — a new category of lender steps in and prices the risk accordingly. The record fund size tells you the demand is real. The KKR backing tells you the smart money thinks the supply of distressed borrowers isn't going away soon.

Saba is not betting on private equity. It is betting on the calendar.

  • Saba Capital doubled its stake in HarbourVest Global Private Equity — a £2.3bn listed fund-of-funds — to 10% via swap derivatives, not physical shares, one week before a shareholder continuation vote on July 15
  • HVPE's board has already agreed to a $400m tender offer in autumn, following a £53.4m co-investment sale and a £224m divestment of fund stakes completed under activist pressure
  • Asset Value Investors, a separate activist holding 3.1% of HVPE, is also pushing for the discount — currently around 25% to net asset value — to close

The take

Boaz Weinstein's Saba Capital just doubled its position in HarbourVest Global Private Equity to 10% a week before the £2.3bn listed fund-of-funds holds its first-ever continuation vote — a shareholder vote on whether the trust keeps operating in its current form. Saba built most of that stake through swaps rather than physical shares, which tells you this isn't a long-term conviction bet on private equity. It's a discount arbitrage, and the mechanics are what make it worth your attention.

Here's the tell everyone reading the headline misses: HVPE is expected to win the vote. Saba isn't buying to lose. It's buying because the board has already capitulated in advance — £53.4m of co-investment sales, a £224m fund-stake divestment, and a $400m tender offer teed up for autumn. That tender is the trade. Saba doesn't need to force a wind-down; it needs a large, near-certain liquidity event at or near net asset value against a stock it accumulated at a 25% discount. Buy the pound for 75 pence, tender it back to the company for something close to a pound. The continuation vote is theatre. The tender is the cash register.

This is the structural story that UK-listed private assets still haven't internalized. The listed private equity trust is a broken instrument — daily-liquid equity wrapped around illiquid, slow-marking fund interests — and that mismatch produces persistent double-digit discounts that no amount of buyback signalling closes. Saba figured out that you don't argue the merits of the discount, you weaponize the governance calendar. Continuation votes, board removals, tender offers: these are all forced-liquidity triggers, and a 10% swap position gives you the votes to pull them without tying up much balance sheet. Weinstein has now run this playbook on Edinburgh Worldwide, Impax Environmental, and a dozen others. HVPE isn't a new campaign. It's the same machine pointed at the fattest remaining discount.

The read-across for allocators is uncomfortable. Every LP sitting on a UK-listed PE trust as a "cheap way to get private markets beta" is holding an asset whose price is now set by activists trading the governance structure, not by the underlying portfolio's DPI. The NAV is real; your ability to realize it depends on whether someone like Saba shows up to force the exit — and when they do, they capture the discount closure, not you. If you own these trusts, you are effectively short a governance option that Boaz Weinstein is long.

The second-order effect nobody is pricing: this pressure permanently narrows the future for listed private equity in London. Boards will pre-emptively shrink, tender, and de-risk to avoid becoming targets, which starves the vehicles of scale and drives the genuinely long-duration capital back into unlisted structures. Saba isn't just harvesting discounts. It's quietly euthanizing the listed-PE format.

The other side

HVPE's board has done enough to win the continuation vote on its own terms, and if it passes cleanly, Saba's leverage disappears. The $400m tender offer is capped and oversubscribed risk is real — if enough shareholders tender, Saba's actual exit at close-to-NAV gets pro-rated down, and the residual position sits in a trust that just survived its governance crisis and has less pressure to act again. Saba has also accumulated through swaps rather than outright shares, meaning its economic exposure and voting mechanics may not align perfectly, and a determined board could contest the influence of derivative-held positions in a Guernsey-domiciled vehicle.

The mechanism Saba is running here is worth slowing down on, because it is not particularly complicated once you see it, but most retail holders of these trusts have never framed it this way. A listed private equity trust — a closed-end fund trading on a stock exchange that holds interests in private equity funds underneath — almost always trades at a discount to its net asset value. The discount exists because the underlying assets are illiquid, slow to mark, and hard to exit on demand. Investors applying a daily-liquid equity wrapper to those assets rationally demand a discount as compensation for the mismatch. That discount has historically sat anywhere from 10% to 35% across the UK-listed PE sector, and no amount of performance reporting or buyback signalling has reliably closed it for long.

What Saba, run by Boaz Weinstein — a former Deutsche Bank credit trader who built his reputation on structured credit before pivoting to closed-end fund activism — figured out is that the discount is not a valuation problem. It is a governance problem. If you can force a liquidity event at or near NAV, the discount collapses to zero on the day of that event. The question is purely mechanical: what lever triggers the event, and how cheaply can you accumulate enough influence to pull it?

The answer in the UK investment trust structure is the continuation vote. Under FCA and Companies Act rules, many UK-listed trusts are required to hold periodic votes asking shareholders whether the fund should continue operating. A no vote triggers a wind-down or restructuring at something approaching NAV. Boards have historically treated these votes as formalities. Saba identified them as loaded weapons sitting unattended on the table.

The playbook has now been executed enough times — Edinburgh Worldwide, Impax Environmental Markets, and roughly a dozen other UK trusts — that boards in the sector have effectively pre-surrendered. They announce tender offers, asset sales, and capital returns before the vote, hoping to neutralize the activist threat without losing the vote itself. This is exactly what HVPE has done. The irony is that by doing so, they hand Saba the trade anyway. Saba accumulates at a 25% discount, the board announces $400m of liquidity to defend itself, and Saba tenders at close to NAV. The activist does not need to win the vote. The threat of the vote does the work.

The longer-run consequence for the asset class is structural and probably irreversible. UK-listed private equity vehicles only make sense at scale — they need large, patient capital bases to absorb the J-curve of underlying fund investments and deliver the illiquidity premium over time. Activist pressure forces boards to shrink via tenders and disposals, which reduces the vehicle's scale, raises its fixed-cost ratio, and makes it a less efficient wrapper for the next pound of capital. The genuinely long-duration LP, the one who actually wants 10-year private markets exposure through a liquid vehicle, gets crowded out by the governance traders. Over time, the listed PE trust ceases to be a viable product, not because the underlying assets performed badly, but because the structure became a target. Allocators who relied on these trusts as accessible private markets exposure are watching the format slowly get arbitraged out of existence.

The take

Anthropic filed confidentially for an IPO on June 1st, and SemiAnalysis — whose bottom-up token-level financial models have been validated by the Wall Street Journal against actual lab numbers — now estimates the company crossed $1B in quarterly profit in 3Q26. That last part is what makes this notable. An AI lab of this scale posting a profit at all breaks the entire "these companies burn cash forever" narrative, and it does so while OpenAI reportedly pushes its own IPO to 2027. The subtext everyone should read: Anthropic isn't going public because it needs the money. It's going public to force OpenAI to show its cards.

Here's the second-order move nobody's pricing. Anthropic filing first is an act of financial warfare, not a fundraise. The moment Anthropic's S-1 becomes public, every OpenAI investor, every hyperscaler underwriting Sam Altman's compute commitments, and every sovereign fund weighing an OpenAI secondary gets a clean, audited benchmark for what a profitable frontier lab looks like. OpenAI then has two bad options: match the disclosure and reveal how much it's still burning, or stay dark and let the market assume the worst. Amodei is using the IPO calendar as a weapon to make OpenAI dance — and the impetus now sits entirely on Altman.

The reason this works is Claude Code. Anthropic won the one AI vertical that monetizes cleanly — enterprise software development — where the customer measures output in shipped code, not vibes, and will pay premium token prices because the productivity delta is legible on an engineering P&L. That's a structurally better business than consumer chat, which is a customer-acquisition-cost bonfire that Google and Meta can subsidize to zero. Anthropic monetizes a workflow; OpenAI monetizes attention. Only one of those throws off $1B a quarter.

For the ICONIQ VII position, the read is straightforward: the mark is about to get validated by a public market, and the SemiAnalysis $6T base case — however aggressive — is the kind of number that makes a late secondary look cheap rather than frothy. The real risk isn't valuation, it's the same knife I've flagged before: Chinese open-weight models undercutting the token price on commodity tasks. Zhipu and MiniMax already IPO'd this year. Anthropic's moat is the premium tier where capability delta still justifies price — and Claude Code is exactly that tier.

The thing to watch is timing. A confidential filing on June 1st with 3Q26 profitability means the roadshow lands into a market where OpenAI is conspicuously absent and hyperscaler equity raises are competing for the same capital. Anthropic isn't just first to file. It's first to define what a profitable AI lab is worth — and that definition becomes the ceiling everyone else has to climb.

The other side

The $1B profit figure comes from a bottom-up financial model, not audited statements — and the gap between a well-constructed estimate and the real number can be large enough to matter when public market investors are doing diligence at scale. More structurally, Anthropic's profitability may be partly a function of timing: Claude Code landed in a window before every major software shop had a capable in-house or open-source alternative. If Meta's Llama or a well-resourced Chinese open-weight model commoditizes the coding-assistant tier within 12 to 18 months, the premium token pricing that makes the margin math work could compress faster than an IPO lockup expires.

The deeper point about Claude Code is that it solved the hardest problem in AI monetization: getting a business to pay recurring, measurable, and growing amounts for AI output because the return is legible on a spreadsheet. Consumer AI products — chat interfaces, image generators, creative tools — all face the same structural problem, which is that the customer's willingness to pay is anchored to what Google and Meta are willing to offer for free or near-free to defend their own ecosystems. Enterprise software development has no such subsidy floor. An engineering team that ships 30% more code per quarter can calculate exactly what that's worth, and the CFO will sign the contract. That's why Claude Code throws off real margin while consumer-facing AI continues to be a customer-acquisition-cost war that only the largest balance sheets can fight indefinitely.

The historical parallel that keeps coming to mind is Salesforce versus the on-premise CRM vendors in the early 2000s. Salesforce didn't win because it had better software in every dimension — it won because it had a delivery model that made the value measurable per seat per month, which made the buy decision easy and the renewal rate high. Anthropic has done something similar: it found the enterprise workflow where AI output is measurable per line of code, per pull request, per deployment cycle. That's a fundamentally stickier business than selling tokens into ambiguous knowledge-work use cases.

The IPO timing dynamic is also worth understanding in its full strategic context. When Anthropic's S-1 becomes public, it won't just be a company disclosure — it will function as an industry-wide price discovery event. Every venture investor sitting on an OpenAI secondary position, every sovereign wealth fund that wrote a check into a hyperscaler equity raise tied to OpenAI compute commitments, and every analyst trying to model what frontier AI is actually worth will use Anthropic's audited numbers as the baseline. That's an enormous amount of market framing power to hand to a competitor, which is exactly why Amodei moved first. Altman now has to either match the disclosure on a timeline he didn't choose, or let the silence do the talking.

The number is not obviously wrong. It is, however, the kind of number that makes any late-stage private entry look reasonable in retrospect if execution holds, and that is directly relevant to how the ICONIQ Strategic Partners VII position — ICONIQ being a private investment firm that manages capital for a concentrated group of ultra-high-net-worth families — gets remarked when public comps arrive.

Tech & AI AI Chips China Nvidia

DeepSeek is building its own chips. This is where Nvidia's inference margin goes to die.

  • DeepSeek — the Chinese AI lab whose R1 model erased $600B from Nvidia's market cap in early 2025 — has been quietly working on its own data center chips for roughly a year, per Reuters citing three sources.
  • The chip effort targets inference (serving live queries to users) rather than training (building the model), and is explicitly designed to reduce dependence on both Nvidia and Huawei, which controls about 50% of China's data center chip market.
  • OpenAI and chipmaker Broadcom jointly unveiled their first inference chip, called Jalapeño, just two weeks ago — meaning labs on both sides of the Pacific have independently reached the same build-don't-buy conclusion at nearly the same time.

The take

DeepSeek — the Chinese lab whose R1 model briefly wiped $600B off Nvidia's market cap in early 2025 — is now designing its own inference chips, per Reuters, and has been quietly at it for a year. The framing everyone will reach for is "China route-around": export controls forced a national champion to go vertical. That's true but boring. The more interesting fact is buried in the sourcing — DeepSeek is targeting inference, not training, and explicitly wants to reduce reliance on Huawei as much as on Nvidia.

That second part is the tell nobody is pricing. The consensus map of Chinese AI has Huawei as the sanctioned-off national arsenal, the Ascend chip everyone salutes and standardizes around. But DeepSeek looking past Huawei means the smartest model shop in China has looked at Ascend's software stack and roadmap and decided it would rather build than buy. When your best customer defects to in-house silicon, you don't have a monopoly, you have a captive audience waiting for an exit. Huawei's ~50% data center share is a floor made of no alternatives, not a moat made of quality.

Now overlay the Western mirror. OpenAI and Broadcom shipped Jalapeño two weeks ago; Anthropic is exploring custom silicon; Amazon has Trainium; Google has had TPUs for a decade. The story on both sides of the Pacific is identical, and it is not about geopolitics — it's about the economics of inference. Training is a capex event. Inference is a perpetual gross-margin tax you pay Nvidia on every token forever. Once your model is frozen and you're serving billions of queries, general-purpose GPUs are wildly overspecified for the job. Every serious lab has independently concluded the same thing: you don't rent your cost of goods sold from a supplier taking 75% margin.

So the real read-across for allocators isn't "China catches up." It's that Nvidia's inference dominance is structurally softer than its training dominance, and the entire hyperscaler-plus-lab cohort is now financially motivated to prove it. The $4T question is what share of AI compute is training versus inference at steady state. The bull case for Nvidia assumes training stays enormous forever. The actual trajectory — models get cheaper to train, then get deployed at massive scale — pushes the ratio hard toward inference, which is exactly the segment everyone is building custom chips to escape.

DeepSeek matters here precisely because it's capital-constrained and can't waste a cent. When the poorest serious lab in the room independently reaches the same silicon conclusion as OpenAI and Amazon, that's not a China story. That's the market telling you where Nvidia's margin goes to die.

The other side

Custom silicon is brutally hard to execute, and the graveyard of failed in-house chip projects is long — Microsoft quietly shelved its Athena AI chip program after years of investment, and even Apple spent the better part of a decade before its custom silicon became a genuine competitive weapon. DeepSeek is capital-constrained by design, operating on a fraction of what OpenAI or Google spends, which means it is attempting one of the hardest hardware problems in engineering without the balance sheet to absorb multiple failed tape-outs. If the first generation of chips underperforms or ships late, DeepSeek stays dependent on Huawei's Ascend hardware — a supplier it clearly doesn't trust — and loses the inference cost advantage that is the entire strategic premise.

Say more →

Start with the hardware economics, because they explain everything else. Training a frontier model is expensive but it happens once, or a handful of times. Inference — generating responses to actual user queries at scale — never stops, and the cost compounds with every user you add. A general-purpose GPU like Nvidia's H100 is engineered to handle the full complexity of training: massive floating-point operations, flexible memory hierarchies, programmability across a huge range of tasks. For inference on a mature, frozen model, that flexibility is mostly waste. You are paying for a Swiss Army knife when you need a very fast, very cheap bread knife. Custom inference chips, designed around a specific model's architecture and workload, can deliver the same throughput at a fraction of the power and cost. That is not a speculative thesis — Google has been running this playbook with its Tensor Processing Units for nearly a decade, and the unit economics on Google Search and Google Translate have reflected it.

What is new is the breadth of the cohort now moving in this direction simultaneously. Amazon has Trainium for its AWS cloud business. Microsoft is developing Maia. OpenAI and Broadcom's Jalapeño announcement two weeks ago was notable specifically because OpenAI framed it as an inference chip, not a training chip — they want Apple-style vertical integration over the full stack, and they want the margin that comes with it. Anthropic, the safety-focused AI lab behind the Claude model family, is also in exploratory mode on custom silicon, though it hasn't hit any public milestones yet. The fact that DeepSeek — which runs lean by necessity, not philosophy — arrives at the same destination confirms this is a gravitational pull, not a strategic fashion.

The Huawei dimension deserves more attention than it is getting. Western analysis tends to treat Huawei's Ascend chip as China's sovereign GPU answer, the inevitable default for any Chinese lab locked out of Nvidia. DeepSeek's move suggests the reality is messier. Huawei's hardware market share in China — roughly half of data center chips — is a metric that reflects the absence of alternatives, not customer satisfaction. Ascend's software ecosystem, the tooling developers use to write and optimize code for the chip, is widely considered immature compared to Nvidia's CUDA platform, which has had a decade-plus head start and an enormous developer community built around it. If DeepSeek, the lab with arguably the strongest model-efficiency track record in China, has evaluated Ascend's roadmap and decided it would rather spend scarce capital building from scratch, that is a signal about Huawei's product — not just about DeepSeek's ambitions.

For investors trying to size Nvidia's long-term earnings power, the training-versus-inference ratio at steady state is the number that matters most. Nvidia's current valuation is built on an assumption that demand for its highest-end GPUs remains insatiable. That holds while the industry is in a heavy training cycle — building the next generation of frontier models. But the trajectory of AI economics points toward a world where training becomes cheaper and faster, models get commoditized, and the volume game shifts to inference at massive scale. That is the segment every major lab and hyperscaler is now motivated to own internally. The squeeze on Nvidia's inference business will not happen overnight, but the alignment of incentives across OpenAI, Amazon, Google, and now DeepSeek is about as clear a directional signal as markets produce.

The Fed just stopped telling you what it's going to do. That is the policy.

  • The FOMC voted unanimously to hold the benchmark funds rate at 3.5%–3.75% in June, but the internal split was so clean that 'many participants' wanted rates lower by year-end and 'many other participants' wanted them higher — no majority view disclosed
  • Fed Chair Kevin Warsh's first meeting minutes ran 14 pages and the post-meeting statement was roughly one-third the typical length, with the committee deliberately stripping out the prior easing-bias language that markets had been trading against
  • FOMC participants flagged AI infrastructure buildout as an upward pressure on technology and electricity prices — a direct contradiction of Warsh's own publicly stated view that AI is ultimately disinflationary

The take

The June FOMC minutes, Kevin Warsh's first as Fed chair, landed Wednesday with a deliberate absence of information. Officials split so evenly on rates that "many participants" wanted the funds rate at or below the current 3.5-3.75% by year-end while "many other participants" wanted it above — a genuine deadlock — and Warsh let the ambiguity stand rather than paper over it with forward guidance. Markets barely moved. That non-reaction is the story.

Here's what's actually happening: Warsh is dismantling the Greenspan-through-Powell doctrine that the Fed's job is to pre-commit and hand-hold. The 14-page minutes, the one-third-length statement, the killing of the easing-bias boilerplate, the "family fight" framing — this is not sloppiness, it's a philosophy. Warsh believes forward guidance turned the Fed into a volatility-suppression machine that markets front-ran into every risk asset. He's removing the put. And the consensus read — "gridlocked Fed, no signal, move on" — is exactly the mispricing. The signal is that there will be no signal. That is a structural change to the reaction function, and rates desks priced it as a nothing day.

The second-order effect nobody is discussing: a Fed that refuses to guide raises the term premium and fattens the tails on both sides. When Powell told you the path, you could sell vol against it. Warsh is telling you decisions come from "incoming information" and nothing else, which means every CPI print and every Hormuz headline now carries genuine two-way risk instead of being pre-absorbed into a guided path. For allocators, that argues for owning rate volatility, not shorting it, and for the first time in a decade the convexity is cheap because the street still trades the old regime.

Then there's the AI split, which is the real intellectual fault line buried in these minutes. Participants flagged that "strong demand for AI infrastructure would likely sustain upward pressure on prices for technology products and electricity" — an inflationary read. Warsh personally believes AI is disinflationary via productivity. Both cannot be right on the timeline that matters for 2026 rates. Datacenter buildout and grid strain are a here-and-now cost-push shock; the productivity payoff is a three-to-five-year story. Warsh is betting the future against the committee's present, and if he's early, he holds rates too high into a supply shock the Fed itself is helping fund. That's the trade to watch: the gap between the electricity CPI line and the software-productivity thesis.

The tell is Trump nominated Warsh to cut, and Warsh's first act was to unanimously hold at 3.5-3.75% and strip the easing bias. The man Trump installed to be dovish just took away the market's ability to assume anything. Position for wider ranges, not lower rates.

The other side

Warsh's silence-as-strategy only works if markets eventually reprice to reflect genuine uncertainty, but there is a credible case that the opposite happens: without anchor guidance, long-end Treasury yields drift higher as investors demand more compensation for not knowing the path, which tightens financial conditions independently of anything the FOMC actually votes on. The 10-year yield rising on Wednesday's minutes release is an early data point for exactly that dynamic. If term premium expansion does the Fed's tightening work for it, Warsh ends up with more restriction than he intended and a recession he can't easily communicate his way out of, having already dismantled the communications toolkit.

Say more →

The institutional context matters here. From Alan Greenspan through Jerome Powell, the Fed spent roughly four decades building what economists call the 'reaction function' — a predictable, well-telegraphed link between economic data and policy response that let investors price assets with unusually low uncertainty about the central bank's next move. Forward guidance, dot plots, post-meeting statements loaded with conditional language: all of it was designed to reduce surprise and, by extension, reduce the volatility premium baked into interest rates. The practical effect was that rates volatility — meaning the cost of options that pay off when interest rates move sharply — became chronically cheap, because the Fed was essentially pre-absorbing shocks through communication before they hit markets.

Warsh is deliberately unwinding that. His background as a Fed governor during the 2008 crisis left him openly skeptical that forward guidance does more good than harm. His argument, stated across several speeches before his chairmanship, is that when the Fed pre-commits to a path, sophisticated market participants front-run that path into equities, credit, and housing, inflating asset prices and making the eventual correction worse. By refusing to pre-commit, he forces every participant to do their own forecasting rather than outsourcing it to the FOMC.

The practical consequence for portfolio construction is significant. For most of the past decade, selling rate volatility — collecting premium from investors who wanted insurance against sharp rate moves — was a reliably profitable strategy precisely because the Fed kept the actual range of outcomes narrow. That trade is now structurally less attractive. When the chair tells you explicitly that decisions depend on 'incoming information' and nothing else, the distribution of outcomes widens by design, not by accident.

The macro backdrop makes Warsh's timing either brave or reckless, depending on how the next two quarters resolve. Inflation has been running hot, driven first by Trump's tariff program and then by the Iran conflict's closure of the Strait of Hormuz, a critical chokepoint for global oil shipments. Energy prices have since pulled back, which gives the dovish camp within the FOMC room to argue the shock is transitory. But the hawkish camp can point to the AI infrastructure buildout — the massive, ongoing investment in data centers and the electricity grid capacity to run them — as a durable, demand-driven cost pressure that has nothing to do with tariffs or geopolitics and won't resolve on its own.

What makes that AI fault line particularly sharp is that Warsh himself appears to be on the minority side of his own committee. His public view is that AI productivity gains will eventually be disinflationary, compressing service costs across the economy. That is almost certainly true over a five-to-ten-year horizon. The problem is that the electricity and construction costs from building the infrastructure come now, while the productivity dividend arrives later. A Fed chair who is personally optimistic about the long run may hold rates lower than the near-term data justifies, and the committee's own minutes flagged that risk explicitly.

The political dimension compounds this. Trump nominated Warsh with a clear preference for lower rates, and Warsh's first act was to hold steady and strip out the language markets read as a promise to ease. That is either a show of genuine independence or a slower path to the same destination. Either way, anyone still positioned for a straightforward rate-cut cycle in 2026 is trading a regime that no longer exists.

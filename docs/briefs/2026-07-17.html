ICONIQ Portfolio Companies Anthropic Enterprise AI Private Equity

Anthropic built its distributor. That distributor will eventually replace it.

  • Ode, a $1.5 billion AI implementation joint venture, was built on the acquisition of Fractional AI — a boutique that ended an 11-month OpenAI partnership the day the Anthropic deal closed.
  • Ode currently employs just 100 engineers, over half of them former founders, against a demand for applied AI talent that already outstrips supply across the industry.
  • Blackstone conceived the entire venture after watching large consulting firms and small AI boutiques both fail to wire AI effectively into its portfolio companies.

The take

Anthropic and Blackstone just put a name and a number on something that has been half-built in the open for six months: Ode, a $1.5 billion AI implementation joint venture with Hellman & Friedman and Goldman also on the cap table, built on the acquisition of Fractional AI — an engineering services boutique that walked away from an 11-month OpenAI partnership the day the deal closed. OpenAI has its own version, The Deployment Company. Both labs are now conceding, in structure if not in press release, that shipping better models is not enough to win the enterprise.

The consensus read is that this is a trillion-dollar new category. It isn't. It's a defensive move dressed as an offensive one. Read the tell in what Ode's own chief technologist said: model selection is "one ingredient," like choosing Python over Java, and "not where the majority of calories are spent." That is a lab-adjacent executive telling you the model layer is being commoditized from the inside. If the model were the moat, you wouldn't need to buy a services firm and staff it with ex-founder generalists to prove the model is worth deploying. Anthropic is spending equity to build the thing that makes the model sticky because the model alone isn't sticky.

Here's the second-order effect nobody is pricing. Ode runs "Claude-first" but will use rivals "if needed" — which means the implementation layer, not the lab, ends up owning the customer relationship and the switching decision. Blackstone conceived this because consulting firms and boutiques were doing a bad job wiring AI into its portfolio. Whoever sits in the CEO's office running the top-one-or-two priority transformation controls which model gets called, and can swap it the moment a cheaper open-weight option clears the quality bar. Anthropic thinks it's building distribution. It's building the exact intermediary that will eventually arbitrage it. Blackstone understands this perfectly, which is why Blackstone conceived it and Anthropic merely branded it.

The real capital-allocation story is the labor arithmetic, and it's brutal. Ode has 100 engineers, over half former founders, described internally as "special forces" rather than an army of forward-deployed staff. Demand for these people already outstrips supply. You cannot scale a "special forces" model to a trillion dollars — the moment you industrialize it, you become Accenture, which is already building the same FDE teams at a fraction of the talent cost. Either Ode stays boutique and stays small, or it scales and the margin collapses into the same commodity consulting economics everyone's trying to escape.

The bet worth watching isn't Ode. It's that Blackstone, Goldman, and H&F have quietly positioned themselves to own the arbitrage layer above the labs they're funding — and get paid on both sides.

The other side

The counter here is real: enterprise software history is littered with companies that tried to win on raw product and lost because they underinvested in implementation. Salesforce, Workday, and SAP all became sticky not because their code was irreplaceable but because certified partner ecosystems made switching cost-prohibitive. If Anthropic can make Claude the default substrate that Ode's deployments are engineered around, the switching friction compounds over time — even if a cheaper model exists, ripping out a Claude-native workflow three years in is expensive. A 100-person team today is a 10,000-person partner ecosystem in five years, and at that point the question of which model runs underneath matters a lot less than who trained the implementation layer.

Start with the corporate structure, because it tells you who is really running this. Blackstone — the private equity giant managing over $1 trillion in assets — conceived Ode, not Anthropic. The lab came in later, lent its brand, and provided the Claude-first technical direction. Goldman Sachs and Hellman & Friedman, a San Francisco-based private equity firm known for software buyouts, rounded out the cap table. That is three financial institutions and one AI lab, which means the entity with the clearest fiduciary interest in maximizing returns from the implementation layer is not the entity whose model is being implemented. That misalignment is not an accident — it is the whole game.

The Fractional AI acquisition is the most clarifying data point in this story. Fractional was an AI engineering services startup that had spent eleven months in a formal partnership with OpenAI. The day the Ode deal closed, that relationship ended. No transition period, no dual-running. One inference: Fractional's founders understood that deep lab partnerships and implementation independence are structurally incompatible, and they chose implementation. The fact that they walked away from OpenAI — which has its own version of this play in The Deployment Company — suggests they believed the Ode structure offered more leverage, not just more money.

There is a historical parallel that maps almost exactly onto this. When IBM lost its grip on enterprise computing in the 1990s, the systems integrators — Accenture, EDS, the big consulting arms — stepped into the vacuum and became the actual decision-makers on which hardware and software a Fortune 500 company bought. IBM sold the server; Accenture decided whether it was a Dell or an HP server next cycle. The labs are recreating this dynamic voluntarily and at speed, apparently believing that owning part of the services layer is better than ceding it entirely. Maybe. But Accenture today has a market cap roughly in line with major cloud providers and earns margin on every dollar of technology it recommends — including technology it had no hand in building.

The quality problem is the one that does not get easier with capital. Ode's executives describe their engineers as elite generalist ex-founders who can own a problem end-to-end, navigate enterprise politics, and make defensible architectural decisions under ambiguity. That profile is not a hiring category — it is a career stage. You cannot recruit for it at scale the way you recruit for a specific technical skill. The moment Ode tries to grow from 100 to 1,000 engineers without diluting that standard, it faces a binary: either it trains people into the profile over years, extending the timeline to any serious revenue scale, or it lowers the bar and the quality proposition that justifies the premium evaporates. Accenture's AI practice will absorb the commoditized middle of this market on cost alone. The boutique positioning and the trillion-dollar ambition cannot coexist for long.

The firms on the cap table understand all of this. Private equity at Blackstone's scale has been the hidden buyer of enterprise software for two decades — they know how implementation partners extract value from software vendors, because they have owned both sides of that trade. The most likely outcome is not that Ode becomes a trillion-dollar company. It is that Ode becomes a highly profitable, moderately scaled services business that Blackstone eventually takes public or sells to a strategic acquirer, having used the Claude brand to establish early positioning and the portfolio company pipeline to guarantee initial revenue. Anthropic gets distribution in the short run and a better-capitalized competitor in the medium run. Blackstone gets paid either way.

Tech & AI Semiconductors Geopolitics AI Infrastructure

TSMC's $100 billion Arizona bet is a political ransom payment. The market is calling it a growth story.

  • TSMC announced an additional $100 billion US investment on top of $65 billion already pledged, targeting new fabs and advanced packaging plants in Arizona.
  • The company raised 2026 capital expenditure guidance to $64 billion and full-year revenue growth forecast to 40%, driven by AI chip demand.
  • TSMC founder Morris Chang previously warned Arizona manufacturing would cost 'at least 50% more' than Taiwan — the new commitment doesn't change that math.

The take

TSMC just committed another $100 billion to Arizona, on top of the $65 billion already pledged, while raising 2026 capex to $64 billion and full-year revenue growth guidance to 40%. The consensus read is simple: AI demand is insatiable, TSMC is the picks-and-shovels winner, buy the story. That's half right and dangerously incomplete.

Here's what nobody's pricing. This $100 billion is not a demand signal — it's a political insurance premium, and TSMC is paying it under duress. Look at the geography of the announcement. Fab capacity plus advanced packaging in Arizona is TSMC building a hostage-release clause into Washington: enough of the crown jewels onshore that a Taiwan contingency doesn't zero the US chip supply chain, which is precisely the outcome that lets the Pentagon quietly recalibrate how much it's willing to defend the island itself. Morris Chang said years ago that Arizona fabs would cost "at least 50% more" than Taiwan and that onshoring was "a very expensive exercise in futility." He was right on the economics and he's being overruled on the politics. That gap — the deliberately accepted margin destruction — is the real story, and it doesn't show up in a 40% revenue print.

The second-order effect is what happens to TSMC's structural pricing power. For two decades the entire moat was geographic concentration: one company, one island, one cluster of engineers you could not replicate. Every dollar of Arizona capacity chips away at the scarcity that let TSMC charge Nvidia and Apple whatever it wanted. You are watching a monopolist voluntarily disperse the very asset that made it a monopolist, because a US administration with tariff leverage told it to. When the moat is geography and you're forced to duplicate the geography, the terminal multiple should compress, not expand. The market is applying an AI-growth multiple to a business quietly being converted into a regulated national-security utility.

Watch the customer side for the tell. Nvidia, AMD, Apple — they all now get to negotiate against a Taiwan fab and an Arizona fab, and eventually a US government that has explicit views on where their chips get made. That's optionality moving from supplier to buyer. The same week Nikkei runs "affordable alternatives to Nvidia servers crop up" and CXMT raises $8.5 billion for the largest Chinese chip IPO — the substitution pressure is building on both ends of the stack simultaneously.

The allocator takeaway: don't own TSMC as a pure AI-demand call, because the demand is real but the returns on this incremental $100 billion are politically administered, not market-set. The question isn't whether the fabs get built. It's who captures the margin once they do — and the answer is increasingly the customers and the US Treasury, not the shareholder.

The other side

The bull case here is not naive. A 40% revenue growth print is real, AI compute demand is not slowing, and TSMC remains the only company on earth capable of manufacturing the most advanced chips at scale — Arizona or not. Diversifying geography could also reduce the geopolitical risk discount that has historically suppressed TSMC's multiple relative to US peers, meaning the stock could re-rate upward even if per-fab margins compress slightly. And if a Taiwan contingency ever materialized, a company with zero US footprint would face existential customer flight; the $100 billion could be the price of staying relevant to the biggest buyers on the planet for the next two decades.

Say more →

Start with the cost structure, because that's where the story gets ugly in slow motion. When Morris Chang warned about the 50% cost premium for US fabs back in 2022, he was describing a structural reality: Taiwan's chipmaking ecosystem — the engineers, the suppliers, the institutional knowledge, the local government alignment — took forty years to build and cannot be copied by writing a check. Arizona has a labor market built for real estate and tourism, not photolithography technicians. TSMC has had to import Taiwanese engineers to staff its existing Phoenix fab, which created its own political friction with US labor groups. Now they're scaling that problem by a factor of several, spending $100 billion to replicate infrastructure that exists in Taiwan at roughly two-thirds the cost.

The mechanism of margin destruction is worth spelling out precisely because it's gradual enough that equity markets keep ignoring it. TSMC's return on invested capital for its Taiwan fabs runs in the high teens to low twenties. The Arizona fabs, carrying that 50% cost premium and built partly on political obligation rather than demand optimization, will almost certainly come in well below that. When you layer $165 billion in cumulative US commitments onto a balance sheet and those assets earn structurally lower returns than the base business, you are diluting the blended ROIC of the entire company — the same metric that justifies the premium multiple. The market is not modeling this because it's too busy tracking the revenue line.

There's a useful historical parallel here: AT&T in the mid-twentieth century. For decades it was the dominant infrastructure monopoly in the United States, extracting enormous rents precisely because no one could replicate the network. Then regulators, citing national interest, pushed it to expand service to unprofitable geographies, constrained its pricing, and eventually broke up the structure entirely. TSMC is not AT&T, and antitrust is not the threat here, but the dynamic is the same: a natural monopoly with geographically concentrated assets is being told by governments, under explicit economic pressure, to disperse those assets in ways that serve policy goals rather than shareholder returns. The regulatory capture of a private monopoly by national security logic ends the same way every time — the utility survives, the equity premium does not.

The customer optionality shift is the second-order effect that deserves more attention than it's getting. Right now, Nvidia and Apple have essentially one supplier for leading-edge chips: TSMC in Taiwan. That concentration gives TSMC real pricing leverage — if you need the most advanced node and you need it at scale, you pay what TSMC charges. Once Arizona capacity is fully online, those same customers can credibly argue for geographic diversification in their supply contracts, pit Taiwan pricing against US pricing, and invite US government stakeholders into the conversation about where their chips get made. That is a fundamental rebalancing of negotiating power away from the supplier. Combine that with CXMT — a Chinese memory chipmaker — raising $8.5 billion in what would be the largest Chinese chip IPO ever, and you have substitution pressure building from below at the same time buyer leverage is building from above. TSMC is getting squeezed from both directions simultaneously, and the headline number is 40% revenue growth.

Private Markets Secondaries Liability LP Dynamics

Continuation vehicles are a $106 billion conflict of interest. The claims haven't hit yet.

  • GP-led secondary transactions — deals where a PE firm moves assets from an old fund into a new one it also controls — hit $106 billion in 2025, now comparable in scale to IPOs and strategic sales as an exit route
  • Bain counts 30,000+ unsold portfolio companies in private equity portfolios; PwC estimates it would take roughly nine years to clear that backlog at current exit velocity
  • The SEC is already scrutinizing conflicts, valuations, and investor consent in the private-funds space, and D&O and E&O insurance policies carried by GPs were not priced for a world where the primary exit mechanism is structurally self-dealing

The take

An insurance-industry blog just flagged something the PE marketing decks won't: continuation vehicles, now a $106 billion market in 2025 per PitchBook and roughly on par with IPOs and strategic sales as an exit route, are quietly becoming a litigation and regulatory liability that sponsors have not priced. The trigger for writing about this now isn't the CV boom itself — everyone knows that story. It's the backlog behind it. Bain counts more than 30,000 unsold portfolio companies, nearly 4,000 of them older than six years, 1,500 north of nine years, and PwC's math says clearing the inventory at current exit velocity takes about nine years. That is not a liquidity gap. That is a structurally jammed exit machine, and the CV is the release valve everyone is now leaning on far past its design tolerance.

Here's the tell nobody's saying out loud: the CV is the only trade in private equity where the same party sets both the bid and the ask. The GP represents the selling fund, organizes the buying vehicle, sets the valuation, designs the process, and keeps managing the asset afterward for more fees and reset carry. In any other market that's called self-dealing. In PE it's called liquidity solutions. The fairness opinion, the independent valuation, the LPAC signoff — these are not risk controls, they're litigation insulation manufactured after the decision to hold has already been made. And they only hold up until the outcome is known.

That asymmetry is the second-order effect the industry is ignoring. A CV can only be sued in hindsight, and hindsight cuts both ways. If the asset rips, the exited LPs claim they were shaken out cheap. If it craters, the rolled and new LPs claim they were sold a bag the GP was too conflicted to let go of. There is no clean outcome — every CV is a live claim waiting for the performance data to pick a plaintiff. Multiply that across a decade of 2020-2022 vintage assets being force-marched into new holds because the IPO window won't open, and you have a claims pipeline that matures on a delay.

The read-across for allocators is specific. The SEC is already circling conflicts, valuation, and consent in the private-funds space, and the D&O and E&O policies GPs carry were not underwritten for a world where the flagship exit strategy is structurally conflicted. Price that in two places. First, discount the DPI story of any GP whose "distributions" are really CV roll-ups — that's the sponsor buying its own inventory with fresh LP money, not a sale. Second, when you commit to Fund V, ask what percentage of Fund III's realizations came from CVs. If it's high, you're not underwriting a manager's exit skill. You're underwriting their litigation calendar.

The other side

The bear case on CV liability risk is that the market has been running long enough to develop real guardrails — LP advisory committee sign-offs, third-party fairness opinions, and independent valuation agents are now standard practice on most institutional-quality deals, and plaintiffs still have to clear a high bar to prove a conflicted process caused actual harm rather than just theoretical one. The SEC's enforcement record in the private-funds space, while growing, has largely targeted egregious disclosure failures and outright fraud, not the ordinary tension inherent in any GP-led transaction. If the asset backlog shrinks as rates fall and the IPO window reopens in 2026 or 2027, the pressure driving borderline CV deals may ease before the litigation wave materializes.

Say more →

The deeper structural problem is one of category confusion. The private equity industry has spent two decades training LPs to evaluate managers on IRR — internal rate of return, a time-weighted performance metric that can be gamed by selectively timing realizations. The industry is now, slowly and painfully, migrating toward DPI — distributions to paid-in capital, meaning actual cash returned to investors — as the more honest scorecard. Continuation vehicles sit right at the fault line between those two metrics in a way that should alarm sophisticated allocators.

When a GP moves an asset from Fund III into a new continuation vehicle, the existing LPs in Fund III receive cash — that registers as a distribution and flatters DPI. But the money funding that distribution comes from new investors buying into the CV, often including other funds managed by the same GP. The asset hasn't been sold to an arm's-length buyer at a market-clearing price. It's been shuffled between pockets while the GP collected a transaction fee, reset the carry clock, and called it a realization. DPI that is manufactured this way tells you nothing about the GP's ability to build and exit businesses. It tells you they're good at financial engineering under pressure.

Historically, the closest parallel is the collateralized debt obligation machine that ran from roughly 2004 to 2007, where the same institutions originating loans were also packaging, rating, and distributing them, with each step in the chain insulated from the final outcome by fees collected upfront. Nobody was lying exactly — every disclosure document said what it said — but the incentive structure guaranteed that the people making decisions bore none of the downside. CVs aren't CDOs, but the conflict geometry is recognizable: the party with the most information about asset quality is also the party designing the transaction, pricing it, and collecting fees regardless of outcome.

The liability timeline matters here. Securities litigation and regulatory enforcement in private markets moves slowly. The CVs being structured today on 2020 and 2021 vintage assets — bought at peak multiples, now stuck because buyers won't pay those prices in a higher-rate environment — will be judged by courts and regulators based on outcomes that won't be clear for another three to five years. That delay creates a false sense of safety for sponsors currently executing these transactions. The absence of claims is not evidence of clean process; it's evidence of a lag.

For allocators doing due diligence on a new fund commitment, there are two practical questions worth adding to the standard checklist. First, ask the GP to decompose their realized returns by exit type — strategic sale, IPO, secondary sale, and CV. A manager whose DPI is heavily weighted toward CVs has a fundamentally different risk and skill profile than one who has consistently found arm's-length buyers. Second, ask what LP consent process was used for each CV and whether any limited partner formally objected or chose to exit rather than roll. Objection rates are not publicly disclosed, but a GP who has run multiple CVs without a single LP dissent either has unusually aligned investors or hasn't been tested yet. Those are very different situations, and right now the industry isn't giving you the tools to tell them apart.

Geopolitics Iran Gulf States Hormuz

The Iran ceasefire was always a pause. The Gulf states are the ones who just lost the most.

  • The June 17 interim deal collapsed after US strikes killed at least 35 Iranians and reimposed a naval blockade — Iran's top negotiator declared an 'existential war' and voided all commitments under the memorandum
  • Iran fired cruise missiles and drones at the US Fifth Fleet in Bahrain and a logistics hub in Kuwait, with Kuwait alone downing four cruise missiles and 21 drones in a single day
  • Over $4 trillion in Gulf sovereign wealth — held across funds like Abu Dhabi's ADIA, Saudi Arabia's PIF, Qatar's QIA, and Kuwait's KIA — is structurally exposed to the regional stability assumption now being dismantled in real time

The take

The June 17 interim deal is dead. Iran's negotiator Ghalibaf declared an "existential war" after a fresh round of US strikes killed at least 35 people, Washington reimposed a naval blockade on Iranian ports, disabled a tanker with a Hellfire missile, and Tehran fired back at the Fifth Fleet in Bahrain and a logistics hub in Kuwait. The consensus read is that Trump overplayed his hand and blew up his own ceasefire. That's backwards. The tell is that Vance went on Joe Rogan the same day to say negotiation is "still essential" while Trump refused to give a deadline. That's not a policy split. That's a deliberate good-cop/bad-cop structure designed to keep the pressure on without closing the exit.

Here's what's actually being priced wrong. The market is treating this as a return to the 2019-2020 tanker-war template — episodic, contained, Hormuz spikes that fade in a week. But the structural variable that's changed isn't Iran or the US. It's the Gulf states. In 2019 the GCC quietly wanted Iran punished. This time Bahrain, Kuwait and Jordan are the ones eating Iranian cruise missiles and drones, and the GCC secretary-general is publicly condemning Tehran while privately opposing the US-Israel war that provoked it. Zeidon Alkinani of the Arab Perspectives Institute — a Gulf-focused think tank — put it plainly: Gulf patience "may fall apart very soon." When Riyadh and Abu Dhabi are simultaneously furious at Washington for restarting the war and at Tehran for spraying missiles across their airspace, the diplomatic center that both sides need to broker any deal is gone.

That's the real risk, and it isn't the oil price. Brent will spike on any Hormuz headline and mean-revert as it always has, because everyone knows Iran can't actually close the strait without strangling its own only export customer, China. The mispriced exposure is the Gulf sovereign wealth complex — the $4-plus trillion sitting in ADIA, PIF, QIA, Mubadala and Kuwait's KIA — that has spent five years building its entire post-oil thesis on regional stability, inbound Western capital, and megaprojects like NEOM that require exactly one thing: nobody firing cruise missiles over the peninsula. A low-intensity war with no ceasefire and no broker is a direct tax on that thesis.

The allocator read: this is not a trade, it's a regime. The June deal wasn't a peace, it was a pause, and it's now clear both sides used it to reposition rather than de-escalate. Iraq's PM was in the White House this week trying to deepen economic ties while Iran-backed militias on his own soil threaten US forces — that contradiction tells you the entire region is being forced to pick a side it doesn't want to pick. Underweight anything that assumes Gulf stability is a constant. It just became a variable.

The other side

The good-cop/bad-cop framing assumes coordination that may not exist. Trump has a documented history of undercutting his own negotiators in real time, and Vance going on Joe Rogan to push diplomacy while Trump threatens to knock out Iran's power grid looks less like choreography and more like a White House that doesn't have a unified position. If the pressure campaign isn't actually controlled, Iran has no reliable offramp to find, and the escalation ladder has no agreed top rung — which means the containment logic breaks down and the 2019 tanker-war template undersells the tail risk rather than just mispricin it.

Say more →

Start with the mechanics of the deal that just died. The June 17 memorandum of understanding was explicitly conditional — both sides agreed their obligations held only as long as the other side performed. Iran's Foreign Ministry spokesman said as much word for word when he announced Tehran was walking away. That's not a country rage-quitting a deal; that's a country invoking the exit clause its lawyers built in from the start. Which means the architecture was always fragile by design, constructed to be reversible the moment either side decided the cost of compliance exceeded the cost of confrontation.

The US targeting logic is worth examining closely. Central Command said it struck Iranian command centers, air defense sites, missile and drone capabilities, and coastal surveillance facilities specifically to degrade Iran's ability to threaten shipping in the Strait of Hormuz. That's a military-operational objective, not a coercive diplomacy objective. When you're destroying coastal surveillance infrastructure, you're not sending a message — you're removing capability. Iran read it the same way, which is why Ghalibaf immediately declared complete freedom of action rather than requesting emergency talks.

The Strait of Hormuz piece is overplayed in most coverage, for reasons the take identifies correctly. Iran cannot close Hormuz without cutting off China, which buys roughly 90 percent of Iran's oil exports and has shown no appetite for that disruption. But the fixation on Hormuz has caused most analysts to miss where the real friction is accumulating: inside the Gulf Cooperation Council, the six-nation bloc of Arab monarchies that borders Iran across the Persian Gulf.

Kuwait shooting down 25 Iranian projectiles in one day is not an abstraction for Kuwait's government. These are countries that have spent the last decade building a foreign policy identity around being indispensable neutral ground — hosting US bases while maintaining economic ties with Iran, backing normalization with Israel while keeping Gulf Arab public opinion onside. That balancing act depends entirely on neither side treating the Gulf as a theater. Iran just treated it as a theater.

The practical consequence is that the informal diplomatic back-channels that have historically allowed the US and Iran to signal interest in talks without losing face are now either compromised or actively hostile. Oman has traditionally played that role, quietly facilitating communications that neither Washington nor Tehran could acknowledge publicly. But Oman sits inside a Gulf that just absorbed an Iranian missile barrage. The willingness of any neutral to stick its neck out has just become much harder to sustain.

The second-order effect for institutional capital is underappreciated. Gulf sovereign wealth funds — ADIA, PIF, QIA, Mubadala, and Kuwait's KIA collectively managing over four trillion dollars — have spent five years repositioning away from pure oil dependency toward inbound foreign direct investment, tourism, financial services, and landmark infrastructure. Saudi Arabia's NEOM megacity project alone represents a bet worth hundreds of billions that the northwest Arabian Peninsula is a stable, investable address. A low-intensity war with no ceasefire and no functioning broker does not need to escalate into a regional conflagration to damage that thesis. It just needs to persist long enough to make the risk premium on Gulf exposure feel non-trivial to Western capital allocators who have other options. Persistent uncertainty is the tax, even if the missiles stop tomorrow.
